# Products & Materials Identification Guide

> Reference file for photo analysis during /new-claim workflow.
> Read this when analyzing CompanyCam photos to identify roofing and exterior products.

---

## Asphalt Shingles

### Three-Tab Shingles
**Visual identification**: Flat profile with three evenly spaced cutouts (slots) creating three
individual tabs per shingle strip. Uniform appearance with minimal shadow line. Each tab is
typically 12" wide, with the full shingle strip being 36" wide and 12" exposed.

**Xactimate reference**: Remove $73.14/SQ | Install $312.92/SQ (NYBI26)

**Key manufacturers and signatures**:
- **CertainTeed XT25 / XT30**: Slightly heavier weight, consistent coloring. XT25 was the most
  common 3-tab in the Northeast for decades. **DISCONTINUED by many suppliers** — flag if identified.
- **GAF Royal Sovereign**: Lighter weight 3-tab, often has a slightly chalky texture when aged.
  Still in production but increasingly uncommon in new installations.
- **Owens Corning Supreme**: Recognizable by the OC color palette (Onyx Black, Estate Gray,
  Driftwood). Thinner profile than CertainTeed equivalents.
- **IKO Cambridge (3-tab variants)**: Thinner profile, sometimes shows premature granule loss.
  Known for warranty issues in certain production years.
- **TAMKO Heritage**: Common in Midwest/South markets. Identifiable by tab shape consistency.

**Age indicators for 3-tab**:
- 5-10 years: Minimal granule loss, colors still vivid, tabs lying flat
- 10-15 years: Some granule loss in water channels, slight color fading, edges may begin curling
- 15-20 years: Noticeable granule loss exposing dark mat, tab corners curling, brittle in cold,
  keyways may show wear
- 20-25 years: Significant granule loss, pronounced curling, cracking at tab slots, some missing
  tabs possible, moss/algae growth common
- 25+ years: End of life — severe granule loss, widespread cracking, brittle, shingles breaking
  at fastener points, may see exposed felt/underlayment

**Shingle Exposure & Metric Transition**:
The entire industry transitioned from imperial (5" exposure, 36" × 12") to metric (5-5/8" exposure,
39-3/8" × 13-1/4") sizing. Most manufacturers completed the 3-tab transition by 2000-2010. GAF
discontinued ALL 3-tab products (Royal Sovereign) in 2023. A roof with 5" exposure 3-tabs has
shingles that are discontinued and unmatchable — no current manufacturer produces this exposure.
**Measure the exposure on EVERY 3-tab roof** — 5" is the strongest unmatchability argument because
it's a physical measurement verifiable by tape measure on-site. An adjuster can't argue about
discontinuation dates when the tape measure proves dimensional incompatibility. If carrier suggests
cutting metric shingles to fit: nailing zones won't align, sealant strips misposition, wind
resistance ratings void (ASTM D7158), and no manufacturer will warrant field-cut shingles.
→ Full technical reference: `references/shingle-exposure-repairability.md`

**Claims relevance**: Three-tab shingles are discontinued or dimensionally unmatchable. **First
check: measure exposure.** If 5" — the product is pre-metric and physically incompatible with ALL
currently manufactured shingles (5-5/8" metric). This triggers full slope or full roof replacement.
If exposure measurement is inconclusive, order an NTS (Name That Shingle) report for independent
third-party confirmation. Carrier cannot argue "close enough" when every course compounds a 5/8"
offset — after 10 courses the lines are 6-1/4" apart.

### Laminate / Architectural Shingles
**Visual identification**: Dimensional profile with layered construction creating depth and shadow
lines. Random or staggered pattern. Heavier and thicker than 3-tab. Exposure varies (typically
5-5/8" to 5-7/8").

**Xactimate reference**: Remove $74.94/SQ | Install $337.78/SQ (NYBI26)

**Key manufacturers and signatures**:
- **CertainTeed Landmark / Landmark Pro / Landmark Premium**: Very common. Landmark has a moderate
  shadow line. Landmark Pro has slightly deeper dimension. Landmark Premium has the deepest profile.
  Identifiable by CertainTeed's granule color blends — "Moire Black" has distinctive blue-gray
  undertones, "Weathered Wood" has brown/gray blend.
- **GAF Timberline HDZ / HD / NS**: The most common architectural shingle in America. HDZ has the
  StrikeZone nailing area (wider nail strip). Identifiable by the consistent layered shadow line and
  GAF's proprietary granule colors. "Charcoal" is their most popular — a true dark gray.
  **Timberline NS (Natural Shadow) was discontinued** — flag if identified.
- **Owens Corning Duration / TruDefinition Duration**: Known for their SureNail technology
  (visible fabric strip in nailing area). Slightly thicker than GAF equivalents. "Onyx Black" and
  "Driftwood" are their signature colors.
- **Atlas StormMaster / Pinnacle Pristine**: StormMaster Shake and Slate have impact-resistant
  (Class 4) ratings. Recognizable by heavier weight and rubberized backing. Pinnacle Pristine has
  Scotchgard algae resistance.
- **TAMKO Heritage / Heritage Vintage**: Common in central US markets. Slightly less defined shadow
  lines than GAF or CertainTeed.
- **IKO Cambridge / Dynasty**: Cambridge is their workhorse line. Dynasty has a deeper dimensional
  profile. IKO products have had historical quality issues — note any premature deterioration.

**Designer / Luxury Shingles**:
- **CertainTeed Grand Manor / Carriage House / Presidential Shake**: Heavy, multi-layer construction
  simulating slate or shake. Grand Manor is 3-piece construction. Very expensive to match/replace.
- **GAF Camelot / Slateline / Grand Sequoia**: Camelot simulates slate with a scalloped edge.
  Slateline has a flatter slate appearance. Grand Sequoia is their thickest/heaviest.
- **Owens Corning Berkshire**: Simulates slate. Very distinctive large-format shingle.
- **DaVinci Roofscapes (synthetic)**: Polymer composite simulating slate or shake. Very distinctive
  — lighter weight than real slate but thicker than asphalt. UV-resistant but expensive.

### Impact-Resistant (Class 4) Shingles
Products designed to withstand hail impact per UL 2218 Class 4 testing:
- Atlas StormMaster Shake / Slate
- Owens Corning Duration STORM
- GAF Timberline AH (Armor Hail) / AS II (Armed Shield II)
- CertainTeed Landmark IR (Impact Resistant)
- Malarkey Vista AR (Polymer-modified, naturally impact-resistant)

**Claims relevance**: If a Class 4 product sustains verified hail damage, the storm severity was
significant — document this fact. Many carriers offer premium discounts for Class 4.

## Slate Roofing

### Natural Slate
**Visual identification**: Natural stone with visible cleaving lines and mineral texture. Each
piece is unique. Colors range from black/dark gray to green, purple, red, and mottled combinations.
Thickness varies from 3/16" to 1/2" depending on quarry and grade.

**Xactimate reference**: Slate Custom Blend $4,905/SQ (material only, NYBI26)

**Types**:
- **Vermont Slate (unfading)**: Typically gray, green, or purple. "Unfading" means the color
  doesn't change with weathering. Vermont quarries produce some of the hardest, longest-lasting slate.
- **Pennsylvania Slate (semi-weathering/weathering)**: Often starts dark gray/black and develops
  brown/rust streaks over time as iron content oxidizes. "Weathering" slate is softer and has a
  shorter lifespan (75-100 years vs. 150+ for unfading).
- **Virginia Buckingham Slate**: Dark gray/blue-black. Very dense and durable.
- **Imported slate**: Chinese, Brazilian, Spanish — quality varies widely. Chinese slate has a
  reputation for inconsistent quality. Spanish slate is generally high quality.

**Age and condition indicators**:
- Delamination (layers separating) — indicates soft/weathering slate reaching end of life
- Flaking surface — early stage delamination
- Broken corners — may be impact damage or freeze-thaw cycling
- White mineral deposits (efflorescence) — moisture moving through stone
- Crazing (fine surface cracks) — thermal cycling over decades
- Nail hole cracks — stress from improper installation or expansion/contraction

**Claims relevance**: Slate is extremely expensive. Individual slate replacement is possible if
matching slate is available (same quarry, color, size, thickness). If the quarry is closed or the
slate type is discontinued, matching may be impossible — this triggers significant scope expansion.

### Synthetic Slate
Products like DaVinci, Brava, EcoStar — polymer or rubber composites. Lighter than natural slate,
more uniform in appearance. Identifiable by consistent thickness and uniform color distribution
that's "too perfect" compared to natural stone.

## Metal Roofing

### Standing Seam
**Visual identification**: Clean, vertical raised seams (typically 1" to 1.75" tall) connecting
flat or slightly ribbed panels. Panels are typically 12" to 18" wide. Concealed fastener system —
no visible screws on the panel face.

**Panel types**:
- **Snap-lock**: Panels snap together at the seam. Most common residential type.
- **Mechanical seam (single or double lock)**: Seams are crimped/folded together on-site with a
  seaming tool. More weather-resistant — common in commercial and low-slope applications.
- **Batten seam / T-panel**: Raised cap covers the seam joint. Distinctive wider seam appearance.

**Materials**:
- **Galvalume (steel with aluminum-zinc coating)**: Most common. Smooth, uniform metallic finish.
  Available painted (Kynar/PVDF finish) or bare mill finish.
- **Aluminum**: Lighter, won't rust. Softer than steel — more susceptible to hail denting.
  Used in coastal environments.
- **Copper**: Distinctive green patina develops over time. Starts as bright penny color.
- **Zinc**: Develops blue-gray patina. Less common in residential.

### Exposed Fastener / Screw-Down Metal
**Visual identification**: Visible screws with rubber/neoprene washers at regular intervals.
- **R-panel / PBR panel**: Trapezoidal ribs, most common. Ribs typically 1.25" high, 36" coverage.
- **5V-Crimp**: Five small V-shaped crimps per panel. Common on older or traditional buildings.
- **Corrugated**: Wavy/sinusoidal profile. Classic barn/agricultural roofing.

**Panel gauge reference** (affects dent susceptibility and replacement cost):
- 24-gauge: Heaviest residential — most dent-resistant
- 26-gauge: Standard residential — moderate dent resistance
- 29-gauge: Lightest/cheapest — dents easily from even small hail

**Claims relevance for metal**:
- Standing seam dents may be "cosmetic only" under some policies — check policy for cosmetic
  damage exclusion endorsement. If no endorsement exists, "cosmetic" is not a valid denial.
- If hail cracked the PVDF/Kynar paint coating, it IS functional damage — exposed metal will
  corrode. Document paint fractures at each dent.
- Screw-down panels: check for backed-out screws, deteriorated rubber washers, elongated screw
  holes — these are wear items but affect scope if the panel must be removed.

## Single-Ply Membranes (EPDM, TPO, PVC)

### EPDM (Ethylene Propylene Diene Monomer)
**Visual identification**: Black rubber membrane. Smooth surface. Typically installed in large
sheets with adhesive or mechanically attached. Seams are spliced with contact adhesive or seam tape.

**Age indicators**: Chalking (white powdery surface), shrinkage pulling away from edges and
penetrations, alligatoring (network of fine cracks), surface crazing, seam separation.

**Thickness**: Commonly 45-mil or 60-mil. Thicker = more resilient.

### TPO (Thermoplastic Polyolefin)
**Visual identification**: Typically white or light gray. Smooth surface with visible seams welded
by hot air. Reinforcement scrim may be visible at cut edges. Shinier than EPDM when new.

**Key manufacturers**: Carlisle, Firestone, GAF/Siplast, Johns Manville, Versico.

**Age indicators**: Yellowing/browning, crazing/cracking, seam separation where welds have failed,
brittleness, surface pitting.

### PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)
**Visual identification**: Similar to TPO but slightly glossier. Hot-air welded seams. Often used
in restaurant/commercial kitchen roofing because it resists grease and chemicals.

**Claims relevance for all single-ply**:
- Hail can puncture membranes, especially older/weathered ones — core cuts may be needed to confirm
- Bruising (compressed insulation beneath) is damage even without puncture — reduces R-value and
  creates ponding
- Multiple punctures/widespread bruising typically justifies full membrane replacement
- Patches compromise warranty and waterproofing integrity

## Clay & Concrete Tile

### Ludowici Clay Tile
**Visual identification**: Premier American clay tile manufacturer. Key profiles:
- **Spanish "S" tile**: Classic barrel/S-curve profile. Rich color through the body (not coated).
- **French / flat interlocking tile**: Flatter profile with interlocking edges.
- **Mission/barrel tile**: Two-piece system with half-round covers over flat pans.

**Xactimate reference**: Ludowici Historic tile $5,625/SQ (material only, NYBI26)

**Identification tips**: Ludowici tiles often have the name/logo stamped on the underside. Colors
are through-body (clay itself is colored). Weight: ~900-1100 lbs per square.

**Claims relevance**: Extremely expensive. Many profiles/colors are custom order with 8-16 week
lead times. Broken tiles allow water infiltration at every break point.

### Concrete Tile
**Visual identification**: Heavier than clay, less uniform in color. Surface often textured or has
a slurry coating. Common profiles: flat, low/high-profile S, shake simulation.
Manufacturers: Eagle, Boral, MonierLifetile.

**Age indicators**: Surface coating wear exposing gray concrete, efflorescence, edge chipping,
color fading, moss/lichen growth.

## Cedar Shake & Wood

### Cedar Shake
**Visual identification**: Split or sawn natural wood with visible grain patterns. Irregular
thickness and surface texture. Individual shakes typically 18" or 24" long.

**Types**:
- **Hand-split & resawn**: Split on one face, sawn smooth on the other. Most common.
- **Taper-split**: Tapered by splitting both faces.
- **Straight-split (barn shake)**: Same thickness throughout. Rustic appearance.

**Cedar Shingle** (different from shake): Machine-sawn on both faces, uniform thickness, smoother.

**Age indicators**: Graying/silvering (UV weathering), curling, splitting along grain,
moss/lichen/algae growth, soft spots (rot), missing/displaced shakes, thinning at butt ends.

**Claims relevance**: Increasingly expensive and subject to fire code restrictions. Individual
shake replacement is straightforward IF matching material is available — color matching to
weathered cedar is the challenge. New cedar is golden/reddish-brown; aged cedar is silver-gray.
In fire code zones, damaged cedar may trigger code upgrade to Class A roofing.

## Siding Materials

### Vinyl Siding
**Visual identification**: Plastic panels with woodgrain texture. Horizontal lap (clapboard) most
common. Also board-and-batten, shake/shingle panel, scallop, Dutch lap profiles.

**Xactimate reference** (NYBI26):
- Standard: $7.55/SF | High grade: $7.83/SF | Premium: $8.21/SF
- Insulated: $11.45/SF | Board & batten: $8.94/SF
- Detach & reset: $4.22/SF

**Manufacturers**: CertainTeed, Alside, Mastic (Ply Gem), Norandex, Kaycan, Royal Building Products.

**Identification tips**: Manufacturer stamps on back side or nailing flange. Color runs and profile
dimensions narrow the product. Vinyl fades over time — original color preserved behind fixtures.

**Age indicators**: Color fading (compare exposed vs. protected areas), brittleness (cold cracking),
warping/buckling from heat, chalking (white powder), surface oxidation.

**Claims relevance**: Matching discontinued vinyl colors is nearly impossible because vinyl fades.
Even same product new won't match 10-year-old faded siding. This supports full-side or full-house
replacement under matching arguments. See matching-arguments.md for the full legal framework.

### Aluminum Siding
**Visual identification**: Metal panels, often with embossed woodgrain texture. Thinner and lighter
than steel. Makes a tinny sound when tapped. Paint is baked-on factory finish.

**Xactimate reference**: $12.47/SF (NYBI26)

**Age indicators**: Oxidation/chalking, paint fading, minor denting over years, loose panels.

**Claims relevance**: Largely discontinued for new construction. Matching profile, exposure width,
texture, AND color is extremely difficult. Factory-painted aluminum cannot be field-painted to match.
Strongly supports full-side or full-house replacement arguments.

### Cedar Shake Siding
**Visual identification**: Natural wood shakes or shingles applied as wall cladding. Similar to
roofing cedar but may be thinner. May be stained or painted.

**Xactimate reference**: $18.13/SF (NYBI26)

**Age indicators**: Same as cedar shake roofing — graying, splitting, curling, rot. Painted cedar
shows peeling paint as additional indicator.

**Claims relevance**: New cedar vs. weathered cedar won't color-match. Full-side/full-house
replacement may be warranted for matching.

## Gutters & Downspouts

### Gutter Types
- **K-style (ogee)**: Most common residential. Profile looks like the letter K from the side.
  Available in 5" and 6" widths. Seamless (formed on-site) or sectional.
- **Half-round**: Semicircular profile. More common on historic or high-end homes.
- **Box gutter**: Rectangular, built into roof structure. Common on older/historic buildings.
- **Fascia gutter**: Integrated gutter that replaces the fascia board.

### Gutter Materials
- **Aluminum** (most common): .027" or .032" thickness. Seamless formed on-site.
- **Galvanized steel**: Heavier, eventually rusts. Less common today.
- **Copper**: Premium, develops patina. Very expensive.
- **Vinyl/PVC**: Cheapest. Becomes brittle in cold. Sectional only.

**Xactimate note**: Gutter quantities use 1.6x multiplier (inside + outside runs) per MEMORY.md.

### Downspouts
Standard sizes: 2"x3" (residential) and 3"x4" (larger residential/commercial). Typically
aluminum matching gutter material.

### Claims Relevance
Gutter/downspout damage is the BEST corroborating evidence for hail claims:
- Horizontal and fully exposed — catch everything
- Dent diameter correlates directly with hail stone size
- Dent density supports storm severity argument
- Aluminum dents easily and permanently — undeniable evidence
- ALWAYS photograph gutters on every hail claim, even if the primary claim is for the roof

## Fascia & Trim Metal

### Aluminum Window Wraps (Capping)
**Visual identification**: Aluminum coil stock bent to cover wood window trim. Covers exterior
wood of window frames, sills, headers, and jambs. Typically white or brown.

**Xactimate reference** (NYBI26):
- Standard: $398.11/EA | Small: $256.48/EA | Large: $538.85/EA | XLarge: $652.25/EA

### Fascia Metal
**Visual identification**: Aluminum coil stock covering fascia board along the eave. Extends from
gutter top to under soffit. Usually matches gutter color.

### Soffit
Types: Aluminum (perforated/vented or solid), vinyl, wood (older homes).
Soffit faces DOWN — hail does not typically damage soffit. Wind can detach or buckle panels.

### Claims Relevance
Window wraps are commonly missed by adjusters — ALWAYS check and document. They add scope and
corroborate the claim. Wraps cannot be "un-dented" — replacement is required.

## Aging & Condition Assessment Matrices

### Asphalt Shingle Age Matrix

| Indicator | 0-5 Years | 5-10 Years | 10-15 Years | 15-20 Years | 20+ Years |
|-----------|-----------|------------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| Granule coverage | Full, uniform | Slight loss in high-wear areas | Noticeable loss, some mat showing | Significant loss, dark patches | Severe loss, mat widely visible |
| Flexibility | Pliable | Pliable | Starting to stiffen | Brittle in cold | Brittle year-round |
| Curling | None | None | Slight edge curling | Moderate curling | Pronounced curling/cupping |
| Color | Vivid, uniform | Slight fading | Noticeable fading | Significant fading | Severely faded |
| Seal strip | Fully adhered | Fully adhered | May release in extreme heat/cold | Starting to fail | Frequently failed |
| Algae/moss | None | Possible start | Common | Widespread | Heavy growth |

### Metal Roofing Age Matrix

| Indicator | 0-10 Years | 10-20 Years | 20-30 Years | 30+ Years |
|-----------|-----------|------------|-------------|-----------|
| Paint finish (PVDF) | Like new | Slight chalking | Moderate fading | Significant fading, possible peeling |
| Fasteners (exposed) | Tight, washers intact | Check washers | Washers deteriorating | Washers failed, possible leaks |
| Panel alignment | Perfect | Minimal movement | Some thermal drift | Possible fastener hole elongation |
| Sealant | Fresh | Starting to weather | Cracking | Failed |

## Discontinued Product Detection

### Red Flags
- **Three-tab shingles in non-standard dimensions** (older 3-tabs were not always 12"x36")
- **Color names/blends no longer in manufacturer catalogs** — verify against current websites
- **T-Lock shingles**: Interlocking T-shaped tab cutout. **DISCONTINUED by all manufacturers.**
  Cannot be spot-repaired — full replacement required.
- **Organic-mat shingles**: Cellulose mat instead of fiberglass. **DISCONTINUED by all manufacturers**
  due to premature failure. Identifiable by curling patterns and premature deterioration.
- **Specific discontinued lines**: GAF Timberline NS (Natural Shadow), various CertainTeed Horizon
  and Hallmark lines, Owens Corning Oakridge (older formulations with different dimensions)

### When You Identify a Discontinued Product
1. Document with close-up photos showing profile, tab pattern, color
2. Search manufacturer current catalogs to confirm discontinuation
3. Note in annotations as a matching/repairability issue
4. Recommend ITEL matching report to support full replacement argument
5. Scope for full slope or full roof replacement as appropriate
