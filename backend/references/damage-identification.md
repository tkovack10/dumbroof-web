## Hail Damage — Asphalt Shingles

### Forensic Standards
- **HAAG Engineering:** mat fracture = functional damage; circular indentation + granule displacement = hail
- **ASTM D3462:** standard specification for asphalt shingles made from glass felt
- **ARMA Technical Bulletin:** industry damage assessment criteria

### What Hail Does to Asphalt Shingles
Hail impacts compress the shingle mat, dislodge granules, and fracture the underlying asphalt
and fiberglass reinforcement. The damage may be immediately obvious or subtle — but in ALL cases,
the protective granule layer has been compromised, the waterproof asphalt layer has been
fractured, and the shingle's remaining service life is dramatically reduced.

### Identifying Hail Hits on Asphalt Shingles

**Classic hail damage indicators (use these terms in annotations):**
- **Granule displacement**: Circular or semi-circular area where granules have been knocked loose,
  exposing the dark asphalt mat beneath. Displaced granules may accumulate in gutters or at
  downspout bases.
- **Mat fracture**: The fiberglass mat beneath the granules is bruised, cracked, or fractured at
  the impact point. May not be visible from surface — feel for a soft spot or depression. In cold
  weather, fractured mat is easier to detect because asphalt is less pliable.
  **Per HAAG Engineering, mat fracture constitutes functional damage.**
- **Shiny/dark spots**: Fresh hail hits appear as dark, shiny circles where granules were knocked
  away and fresh asphalt is exposed. Over days/weeks these oxidize and become duller, but the
  granule loss remains.
- **Indentation/depression**: A physical dent or concavity in the shingle surface. More pronounced
  on aged, brittle shingles.
- **Bruising (soft spots)**: Even where granules are NOT displaced, the mat may be fractured
  beneath the surface. Test by pressing with thumb — bruised area feels softer/spongier than
  surrounding undamaged shingle. **This is FUNCTIONAL DAMAGE even without visible granule loss.**
- **Exposed felt/mat fibers**: On harder impacts, the asphalt layer is completely penetrated,
  exposing white or tan fiberglass mat fibers.

### Hail Damage by Shingle Type

**Three-tab shingles** (Xactimate: Remove $73.14/SQ, Install $312.92/SQ — NYBI26):
- Thinner and more rigid than laminate — shows hail damage more readily
- Hits create cleaner, more defined circular marks
- Mat fractures more easily due to single-layer construction
- Granule loss is more immediately visible against the flat, uniform surface
- Aged 3-tab is extremely brittle — hail causes cracking and fracturing beyond just granule loss

**Laminate/architectural shingles** (Xactimate: Remove $74.94/SQ, Install $337.78/SQ — NYBI26):
- Thicker, multi-layer construction absorbs more impact energy
- Hail marks may be less defined because dimensional surface disperses impact
- Look for hits on the exposed tab portion AND on the overlay/laminate layer
- Shadow lines and dimensional texture can make hits harder to spot — use chalk to circle
  confirmed hits during inspection
- GAF Timberline HDZ: StrikeZone nailing strip area is reinforced — hits in this zone may show
  less surface damage but the mat can still be fractured beneath

**Designer/luxury shingles:**
- Heaviest construction — absorbs significant impact before showing surface damage
- Hits may appear as faint impressions rather than clear granule displacement
- Multi-layer construction means damage to top layer may not be visible as easily
- Most expensive to replace — document EVERY hit thoroughly

**Impact-resistant (Class 4) shingles:**
- Designed to withstand hail per UL 2218 testing (2" steel ball drop from 20 feet)
- If a Class 4 shingle shows hail damage, the storm was SEVERE — document this fact
- Damage may appear as surface bruising, granule displacement, or in extreme cases, mat fracture
- Rubberized/modified bitumen backing absorbs more energy — hits show as dimples rather than fractures
- Even Class 4 has limits — large hail (2"+) can still cause functional damage

### Test Square Method
Select representative 10'x10' test squares on each slope and count hail hits. Document:
- Number of hits per square
- Location on roof (slope, distance from ridge/eave, compass direction)
- Size range of hits (measure with ruler or coin comparison)
- Whether hits show granule loss only, mat fracture, or both
- Photo each test square with hits circled in chalk

Most carriers require 8+ functional hits per test square to approve full slope replacement.
The key is that each hit must be FUNCTIONAL damage (mat fracture per HAAG standard, not just
granule disturbance).

### Hail Size Correlation
| Hail Size | Comparison | Impact on Asphalt Shingles |
|---|---|---|
| 1" | Quarter | Can damage aged/brittle shingles and 3-tab; less likely to damage newer laminate |
| 1.25" | Half-dollar | Damages most asphalt shingles regardless of age |

### 1A. Shingle Anatomy & Material Science

Understanding shingle construction at the material level is critical for forensic analysis. Every layer serves a specific purpose, and hail compromises them in predictable ways.

**Layer Structure (bottom to top):**
1. **Bottom asphalt coat** — waterproofing layer applied to the underside of the mat
2. **Fiberglass mat** — non-woven glass fiber web providing tensile strength and dimensional stability. Appears as white threads when exposed. This is the structural backbone of the shingle.
3. **Top asphalt coat** — thicker layer that serves as the granule bed. Granules are pressed into this layer at ~400°F during manufacturing, creating a mechanical bond.
4. **Ceramic granules** — crushed and kiln-fired mineral granules providing UV protection, color, fire resistance, and weather protection. This is the shingle's first line of defense against every element.

**Asphalt Formulation — The Critical Difference:**

| Property | Oxidized Asphalt (Standard) | SBS-Modified Bitumen (Rubberized) |
|---|---|---|
| Manufacturing | Oxygen blown through asphalt to harden | Synthetic rubber polymer (styrene-butadiene-styrene) blended in |
| Flexibility | Rigid, becomes more brittle with age | Remains supple, recovers from deformation |
| Granule Adhesion | Standard — degrades as asphalt oxidizes | **65% greater granule adhesion** (manufacturer data) |
| Tear Strength | Standard | **30% greater tear strength** |
| Hail Response | Fractures — granules displace, mat cracks | Absorbs and rebounds — elastic deformation |
| Aging Behavior | Progressively more brittle, accelerating vulnerability | Maintains flexibility for decades |
| Brands (standard lines) | GAF Timberline HDZ, OC Duration, IKO Cambridge, TAMKO Heritage | — |
| Brands (SBS-modified) | Malarkey (ALL products), select CertainTeed Landmark Pro, Atlas StormMaster | — |

**Why this matters forensically:** SBS-modified shingles absorb hail energy and recover. Oxidized asphalt shingles fracture. The same hail event causes dramatically different damage patterns on different shingle formulations. When a carrier argues "the hail wasn't large enough to cause this damage," shingle formulation is the rebuttal — aged oxidized asphalt fractures at impacts that SBS shingles survive.

### 1B. Impact Physics — The Moment of Strike

**Impact Sequence (milliseconds):**
1. **Surface compression** — hailstone contacts granule surface
2. **Granule crushing** — ceramic granules fracture into small fragments (NOT powder)
3. **Asphalt deformation** — asphalt layer compresses under load
4. **Mat fracture initiation** — fracture initiates on the UNDERSIDE of the mat (tension side) and propagates upward. This is identical to how concrete beams fail — the tension face fractures first.
5. **Rebound** — hailstone bounces away, leaving permanent damage

**Granule Micro-Forensics (Tom Kovack Proprietary Field Expertise):**

This is the single most defensible forensic indicator and the one carriers cannot rebut:

- Downward hail impact **crushes granules into small fragments** (NOT powder, NOT dust) and **embeds those fragments through the asphalt layer into the fiberglass mat strings**
- Under microscope: you can see crushed granule pieces physically pressed into white fiberglass threads. The granule fragments are IN the mat layer.
- Remember the layer order: mat → asphalt → granules. Granule fragments found IN the mat = irrefutable proof of downward external force that drove those fragments through the asphalt
- Surrounding granules are loosened but don't fall off immediately — **visible granule loss becomes more noticeable ~6 months post-storm** as rain and wind wash loosened granules away
- **Hammer/mechanical damage** turns granules to **powder/dust** (pulverizes them). This is completely different from hail's fragment-and-embed pattern. Powder = mechanical. Fragments embedded in mat = hail.
- **Blistering** pushes granules UPWARD from internal gas pressure — it NEVER embeds fragments into the mat from above. The direction of force is the opposite.

**Temperature Effects on Impact:**

| Temperature | Asphalt State | Damage Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| < 50°F | Brittle | Maximum fracture, maximum granule displacement, more severe damage from same hail size |
| 50-70°F | Transitional | Moderate fracture with some elastic recovery |
| > 70°F | Pliable | Elastic deformation, granule embedding, more bruising than fracture |
| **Rain-cooled** | **Worst case** | **Rain cools roof surface making asphalt rigid, then hail strikes rigid surface with maximum granule displacement. Rain immediately washes loosened granules away permanently — destroying evidence.** |

**Critical research data:**
- **Koontz/White study:** 3 of 5 aged shingle specimens fractured at just 1" hail, while new shingles of the same product needed 1.5-2" hail to fracture. Age is the dominant variable in vulnerability.
- **Impact angle:** Koontz/White found no substantial difference in granule loss between 45° and 90° impact angles. Vertical hail = circular marks. Wind-driven hail = teardrop/elliptical marks with the elongation pointing in the wind direction.

**Hail Spatter/Splash Marks:**
- Granule displacement radiating outward from impact point — visible as burnish marks on the asphalt surface
- Each spatter has a "head" (impact point, correlates with hail stone size) and "tail" (extending in wind direction)
- Visible for 1.5-2 years on oxidized asphalt surfaces before UV exposure fades them
- On SBS-modified shingles, spatter marks may be less visible due to elastic recovery

### 1C. Blistering vs. Hail — Deep Forensic Differentiation

**This is the #1 carrier denial tactic.** If a carrier can successfully argue that hail damage is actually blistering, they pay nothing. Master this differentiation.

**The Fundamental Physics:**
- **Hail** = external DOWNWARD force (creates concave dent, depression INTO the shingle)
- **Blistering** = internal UPWARD force (creates convex bubble/crater, pushes material UP from within)
- They are the **complete opposite** in force direction, mechanism, and forensic signature

**Blister Anatomy:**
Gas trapped between asphalt layers during manufacturing → heat (solar gain) expands the gas → asphalt bubbles UPWARD → eventually the bubble pops → crater forms with exposed white fiberglass threads + crusty/raised asphalt ring around the edges + no granules in center. The untrained eye confuses this with hail because both present as "a dark spot with missing granules."

**Hail Anatomy:**
Downward impact → granules crushed into fragments and embedded through asphalt INTO fiberglass mat → surrounding granules loosened → mat fractured/bruised beneath surface → slight depression/concavity at impact point

**12-Point Forensic Differentiation Checklist:**

| # | Indicator | Hail Damage | Blistering | Mechanical (Hammer/Tool) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Force direction** | Downward (external) | Upward (internal) | Downward (external) |
| 2 | **Surface profile** | Concave depression | Convex raised or crater with raised edges | Sharp depression, may show tool mark |
| 3 | **Edge character** | Smooth, blends into surrounding surface | Crusty, raised, volcano-like ring | Sharp, defined edges |
| 4 | **Granule state** | Crushed into fragments, embedded in mat | Pushed aside/upward, intact | Pulverized to powder/dust |
| 5 | **Mat fracture** | Yes — fractured on tension (underside) first | No fracture — mat stretched/opened | Yes — but from concentrated point load |
| 6 | **Fiberglass exposure** | Mat fractured beneath surface (feel as soft spot) | White threads exposed FROM BELOW (bubble opened layers) | Mat may be punctured from above |
| 7 | **Shape** | Circular/oval, consistent with hailstone size | Irregular, follows trapped gas pocket shape | Irregular, may show tool outline |
| 8 | **Size consistency** | Varies within a range matching storm hail data | Random sizes, no storm correlation | Variable |
| 9 | **Distribution** | Random within directional pattern (windward > leeward) | Concentrates on south/west exposures (maximum heat) | Concentrated on accessible areas |
| 10 | **Soft metal corroboration** | YES — gutters, downspouts, wraps also dented | NO — blistering only affects shingles | NO — no collateral damage |
| 11 | **Weather correlation** | Matches documented storm event date | No storm correlation | No storm correlation |
| 12 | **Microscope test** | Granule fragments embedded IN fiberglass mat | No granule fragments in mat | Granule powder (not fragments) |

**Dead Giveaways:**
- **Blisters:** White fiberglass threads exposed FROM BELOW — the bubble opened the layers from inside. Crusty raised edges around crater.
- **Hail:** Mat fractured BENEATH surface — feel by pressing with thumb, area feels spongy/soft. Crushed granule fragments embedded in fiberglass strings visible under microscope.
- **Hammer:** Granules turned to POWDER (not fragments). No collateral damage on surrounding surfaces.

### 1D. The Evidence Cascade — Crime Scene Methodology (Tom Kovack Strategy)

**Core Principle: The adjuster must be convinced hail hit the property BEFORE climbing the ladder.**

Build evidence sequentially. Each layer eliminates a carrier escape route. By the time you reach the roof, the adjuster has already acknowledged hail occurred — they're just documenting scope.

#### Step 1: Environmental / Living Evidence
Evidence that can't be faked and timestamps the event:
- **Holes in large-leaf landscaping plants** — hostas, rhubarb, squash leaves, elephant ear. These heal over time, providing a rough timestamp.
- **Hail spatter on horizontal surfaces** — patio tables, deck railings, concrete walkways, painted handrails. Oxidized burnish marks from granule/debris splash.
- **Tree damage** — broken small branches, leaf shredding on broad-leaf trees, bark scarring on young trees
- **Staining on concrete** — granule wash-off from roof creates staining pattern at drip line

#### Step 2: Soft Metal Chalk Test — MANDATORY

**Every soft metal surface gets chalked. No exceptions.**

**Chalk Technique:**
1. Lay carpenter's chalk **sideways** against the metal surface
2. Draw across with light, even pressure in one direction
3. Chalk **skips over dents** — dents appear as unmarked circles against the chalked background
4. Where chalk stops in a photo, the same dents become nearly invisible — this proves chalk isn't creating false evidence, it's revealing real evidence that blends into the surface without enhancement

**Complete Surface Checklist — ALL Must Be Chalked:**
- [ ] Gutters — front face, top lip, inside trough
- [ ] Downspouts — windward face (face pointing toward storm direction)
- [ ] Window wraps — sills (most exposed), jambs, headers
- [ ] Fascia metal — vertical exposed face
- [ ] Chimney flashing — step flashing, counter flashing, cap
- [ ] Valley metal — if open metal valley
- [ ] Attic fan covers — top surface
- [ ] Pipe boot collars — the metal ring/base
- [ ] Drip edge — exposed face
- [ ] Exhaust vent caps — top and windward face
- [ ] Aluminum siding (if present) — windward elevation

**Dual Purpose of Chalk Protocol:**
1. **Proves hail occurred** — chalked dents are undeniable, measurable, photographable
2. **Adds scope dollars** — most policies do NOT have cosmetic/soft metal exclusions. ALL soft metal damage (functional AND cosmetic) is covered = additional line items that carriers would otherwise overlook

**Without chalk**, dents blend into the aluminum surface and adjusters write "minimal soft metal damage." **With chalk**, every dent becomes obvious in photographs. This is the single highest-ROI inspection technique.

#### Step 3: Directional Pattern
- Windward elevations show more damage than leeward
- Compare ALL four sides — the differential proves directionality
- Cross-reference with HailTrace/NWS storm direction data
- Document with photos of each elevation's soft metals for comparison

#### Step 4: The Logical Trap — Carrier Credibility Destroyed
Once the carrier acknowledges soft metal damage = hail, they cannot credibly deny the roof:
- "You agree hail hit the gutters 8 feet below the roof... but somehow missed the roof itself?"
- "You agree there is hail damage to the downspout and roof vent, but those marks on the roof with very similar characteristics are what exactly?"
- "Your report confirms hail damage to the window wraps at 4 feet off the ground, the gutters at 10 feet, but the roof at 12 feet was somehow exempt?"

The obvious, chalked soft metal damage makes the carrier's roof denial argument collapse under its own logic.

#### Step 5: Roof Evidence — Adjuster Already Convinced, Now Documenting Scope
- Test squares with chalk-circled hits (8+ per 100 sq ft = replacement per most carrier thresholds)
- Microscope showing crushed granule fragments embedded in fiberglass mat
- Compare windward vs. leeward slope density (should match directional pattern from Step 3)
- Document both functional (mat fracture) AND cosmetic (granule displacement without mat fracture) damage
- Close-ups with ruler/coin for scale reference

### 1E. The 1-3 Year Time Bomb — Why Documentation NOW Matters

Hail damage to asphalt shingles is engineered NOT to leak immediately. The shingle system has multiple redundant waterproofing layers. A single hail impact compromises the top layers but the underlayment, ice & water barrier, and asphalt substrate continue to protect — temporarily.

**Tom's line:** "If it's hail damage, it's not supposed to leak... yet."

**Progressive Failure Timeline:**

| Timeframe | What's Happening | Visible Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Day 1** | Impact → crushed/loosened granules, fractured mat | Dark spot at impact, may feel soft |
| **Months 1-6** | Loosened granules wash away from rain/wind | Granule loss becomes visible, dark patches growing |
| **Months 6-12** | Exposed asphalt oxidizes, UV degradation accelerates exponentially | Graying/whitening at exposed areas, asphalt surface roughening |
| **Year 1-2** | Asphalt erodes at exposed areas, mat fibers begin showing through | White fiberglass threads visible, surface erosion, cracking |
| **Year 2-3** | Water penetrates through fractured mat → **leak manifests** | Interior water damage: ceiling stains, mold, insulation damage |

**IBHS Data:** Damaged shingles are **10x more susceptible** to damage from the NEXT hail event. Each subsequent storm compounds the original damage.

**Why carriers delay:** They are incentivized to deny early. If they can stall past the claim filing window (typically 1-2 years depending on state), the homeowner absorbs the full replacement cost. The carrier saves $15,000-$40,000 per denied claim.

**Why forensic documentation at time of discovery is critical:** The evidence degrades. Spatter marks fade. Loosened granules wash away. The shingle surface oxidizes uniformly, masking the original impact boundaries. By year 2, a carrier can more credibly argue "wear and tear" because the evidence of the specific impact event has degraded.

### 1F. Forensic Aging of Hail Hits

Experienced forensic inspectors can estimate the age of hail damage based on visual indicators. This is critical when carriers argue damage is "pre-existing" or from a different storm event.

**Visual Aging Timeline:**

| Age | Appearance | Key Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **0-7 days** | Sharp, dark black exposed asphalt. Fresh, distinct boundaries. | Highest contrast against surrounding granules. Spatter marks crisp. |
| **1-4 weeks** | Slightly less sharp. Beginning of very early oxidation. | Still clearly fresh. No color change yet. |
| **1-3 months** | Edges softening slightly. Early oxidation beginning. | Spatter marks still clearly visible. Granule fragments still loose around impact. |
| **6 months** | Noticeable graying at exposed asphalt. Granule loss has expanded. | Loosened granules have washed away. Impact area larger than initial strike. |
| **1 year** | Gray/weathered appearance at impact. Boundaries less defined. | Oxidation state noticeably different from surrounding aged surface. |
| **2-5 years** | Impact blending into surrounding surface. Mat exposure beginning. | Spatter marks faded or gone. Only depression and granule loss pattern remain. |
| **5+ years** | Hard to distinguish from wear without microscope. | Requires microscope for granule fragment analysis, comparison of oxidation states at impact vs. surrounding surface. |

**Dating Methods:**
1. **Burnish mark analysis** (Nelson Forensics methodology) — measure oxidation state of exposed asphalt at impact vs. unimpacted surface
2. **Oxidation state comparison** — photograph under consistent lighting, compare UV degradation progression
3. **Spatter mark freshness** — presence and sharpness of spatter marks narrows the window
4. **Collateral cross-referencing** — soft metal dent oxidation states should match roof damage age
5. **Weather data correlation** — HailTrace/NWS records confirm storm events on specific dates. Match aging estimate to known storm date.

### 1H. Carrier Denial Rebuttals — Hail-Specific

#### "That's blistering, not hail"
**Rebuttal framework:**
1. Direction of force: The observed damage shows concave depression (downward force). Blistering creates convex raised areas or craters with raised edges (upward force). These are opposite mechanisms.
2. Microscope evidence: Crushed granule fragments are embedded within the fiberglass mat. Blistering cannot embed granule fragments from above into the mat below — it pushes material upward.
3. Mat fracture: The mat is fractured on the tension (under) side. Blistering stretches the mat but does not create tension fracture from external load.
4. Soft metal corroboration: [Count] dents documented on gutters, downspouts, and window wraps at this property. Blistering only affects shingles — it cannot dent metal.
5. Directional pattern: Damage concentration follows windward-to-leeward pattern consistent with [direction] storm documented by HailTrace on [date]. Blistering concentrates on south/west exposures from heat, not storm directionality.
6. Reference the 12-point differentiation checklist (Section 1C) for comprehensive rebuttal.

#### "That's mechanical damage (foot traffic, tools)"
**Rebuttal framework:**
1. Granule analysis: Mechanical damage pulverizes granules to fine powder/dust. The observed damage shows granule fragments (not powder) embedded in the mat — consistent with hail, not mechanical impact.
2. Distribution pattern: Mechanical damage concentrates along access paths (near ladder placement, around HVAC units, along ridge walk paths). The observed damage is randomly distributed within a directional pattern across the entire roof — consistent with falling hail.
3. Shape consistency: Mechanical damage creates irregular marks reflecting the tool/boot shape. The observed marks are circular to oval with sizes consistent with the [X]" hail documented by HailTrace.
4. Soft metal corroboration: The same dent pattern observed on gutters, downspouts, and window wraps rules out foot traffic — nobody walked on the gutters.
5. No access evidence: No ladder marks, no boot scuffs, no path-concentrated damage pattern.

#### "That's wear and tear / normal aging"
**Rebuttal framework:**
1. Granule loss pattern: Normal aging causes diffuse, gradual granule erosion in water channels and UV-exposed areas. The observed granule loss is in distinct circular patterns at impact points — not consistent with gradual erosion.
2. Oxidation state: The exposed asphalt at impact points shows a different oxidation state than the surrounding aged surface — this is consistent with recent damage exposing fresh asphalt on an aged roof.
3. Storm correlation: HailTrace confirms [size] hail on [date] at this location. The damage pattern (directional concentration, size consistency) correlates with this documented event.
4. Soft metal corroboration: [Count] dents on gutters, downspouts, and wraps. Wear and tear cannot dent aluminum.
5. **Pre-existing wear does NOT negate covered storm damage.** A 15-year-old roof can still sustain covered hail damage. The carrier must pay for storm damage regardless of pre-existing condition.

#### "The damage is cosmetic only"
**Rebuttal framework:**
1. **Mat fracture documented:** Per HAAG Engineering standards, mat fracture constitutes FUNCTIONAL damage. [Count] impacts in test squares show mat fracture confirmed by physical palpation (soft spot test) and/or microscope.
2. **IBHS data:** Previously damaged shingles are 10x more susceptible to damage from the next hail event. Each subsequent storm will cause accelerating degradation. This is not cosmetic — this is progressive functional failure.
3. **The 1-3 year time bomb:** Hail-damaged shingles are engineered NOT to leak immediately. Granules protect asphalt from UV → granule loss exposes asphalt → UV degrades asphalt → mat fibers exposed → water intrusion. Leak manifests 1-3 years post-impact.
4. **Manufacturer warranties:** Most manufacturer warranties are voided by documented hail impact. The shingle can no longer be warranted as functional.
5. **Legal precedent:** Seventh Circuit and multiple state courts have ruled that damage to protective systems (granules protecting asphalt, paint protecting metal) constitutes functional damage even when no immediate leak exists.
6. **Policy language:** Review the actual policy — most do NOT contain a "cosmetic damage" exclusion for roofing. If the policy doesn't specifically exclude cosmetic damage, the carrier must pay.

#### "The damage is pre-existing / from a different storm"
**Rebuttal framework:**
1. Forensic aging analysis: The oxidation state, spatter mark freshness, and granule displacement characteristics are consistent with damage from [date range], matching the claimed storm event.
2. Burnish mark analysis: The exposed asphalt oxidation at impact points dates to approximately [timeframe], which aligns with the [date] storm event documented by HailTrace/NWS.
3. Soft metal correlation: Oxidation state of dents on soft metals is consistent with the same timeframe as the roof damage.
4. Weather data: HailTrace confirms [size] hail on [date] at this location. No other significant hail events within the damage aging window.
5. Prior inspection records: [If available] Prior inspection report from [date] documented roof in sound condition with no hail damage.

#### "Functional damage only — we only pay for functional damage"
**Rebuttal framework:**
1. "Functional damage" is not a policy term of art — it's a carrier-imposed limitation that courts have repeatedly rejected when the policy language doesn't support it.
2. The policy provides for repair or replacement to restore the property to pre-loss condition. Granule displacement, regardless of whether the mat is fractured, reduces the shingle below pre-loss condition.
3. Every hail impact compromises the protective granule system, accelerating UV degradation and reducing remaining service life. This IS functional damage to the protective system.
4. Per IBHS, previously impacted shingles are 10x more vulnerable to subsequent storms. Leaving cosmetically damaged shingles in place creates compounding risk of failure.

## Hail Damage — Metal Roofing

### Standing Seam Metal
**What to look for:**
- Dents on flat panel face between seams
- Measure dent diameter and depth — use depth gauge if available
- Check if PVDF/Kynar paint finish is cracked at the dent — run fingernail across dent center.
  If you feel a ridge or paint flakes, the paint system is compromised
  (**This is FUNCTIONAL damage — exposed metal WILL corrode**)
- Standing seam panels show dents more clearly on lighter colors due to shadow/reflection

**Paint fracture vs. dent only:**
- Dent with intact paint: Carrier may argue "cosmetic only" — check policy for cosmetic exclusion
- Dent with cracked/fractured paint: **FUNCTIONAL damage regardless of cosmetic exclusion.**
  The paint system (primer + topcoat) is the corrosion protection. Once breached, steel substrate
  is exposed to oxidation.
- Document paint fractures with close-up macro photos and describe paint condition at each dent

**Panel gauge matters** (thinner = more dents = stronger claim):
- 24-gauge: Heaviest residential — most dent-resistant. Xactimate: $6.72/SF (NYBI26)
- 26-gauge: Standard residential — moderate resistance. Xactimate: $5.45/SF (NYBI26)
- 29-gauge: Lightest/cheapest — dents easily. Xactimate: $4.28/SF (NYBI26)

### Exposed Fastener / Screw-Down Metal
**What to look for:**
- Dents on flat areas between ribs
- Dents on rib peaks (less common but possible with large hail)
- Check rubber/neoprene washers on exposed screws — hail can crack these
- Note backed-out screws (thermal cycling, not hail — but worth documenting)
- Elongated screw holes (thermal movement, not hail — but affects repairability)

**R-panel, 5V-crimp, corrugated:**
- Thinner gauge panels (typically 26-29 gauge) show dents more readily
- Corrugated: dents appear on the crown of each corrugation facing hail direction
- Dent pattern should correlate with storm's wind direction

### Gutters as Corroborating Evidence for Metal Roof Claims
Aluminum gutters dent far more easily than steel roofing panels. Extensive gutter denting
corroborates hail — even if steel panels show minimal damage. The ABSENCE of gutter damage
casts doubt on hail claims to metal roofs.

## Hail Damage — Slate

### Natural Slate
(Xactimate: Custom Blend $4,905/SQ material only — NYBI26)

**What to look for:**
- **Cracks**: Star-pattern cracks or linear fractures from hail strikes. May be immediately
  obvious or hairline-thin.
- **Chips/spalls**: Corners or edges chipped from impact. Fresh break is LIGHTER colored;
  weathered break is same color as surface.
- **Punctures/holes**: Large hail can punch through thinner slate (3/16"-1/4").
- **Surface divots**: Impact marks creating concave divots. Fresh divots have sharp edges and
  lighter-colored exposed stone.

**Distinguishing hail damage from wear:**
| Indicator | Hail Damage | Natural Wear |
|---|---|---|
| Break edges | Fresh, sharp, lighter-colored | Weathered, rounded, same color |
| Distribution | Random, correlated with storm direction | Uniform delamination |
| Cause pattern | Impact point visible | Layered flaking from freeze-thaw |
| Nail stress | Not related to nail holes | Cracks radiating from nail holes |
| Walking damage | Random, not on paths | Follows walking paths near edges/equipment |

**Claims significance:**
- Even a single cracked slate is a leak point — underlayment below may be decades old
- If slate type/quarry is discontinued, matching individual replacements is impossible
- Photo each damaged slate: close-up of crack/chip AND wide shot of location on roof

### Synthetic Slate
- Hail can crack, chip, or puncture synthetic panels
- Polymer-based synthetics may show stress whitening at impact points
- Matching may be easier than natural (manufactured product) but color matching to UV-faded
  existing panels is still a concern

## Hail Damage — Single-Ply Membranes

### EPDM
**What to look for:**
- **Punctures**: Especially on aged/chalked membrane. May appear as small holes or tears.
- **Bruising/indentation**: Underlying insulation board (polyiso, EPS) compressed at impact.
  Even if membrane isn't punctured, compressed insulation has reduced R-value and creates a
  low spot that ponds water.
- **Membrane fracture**: In cold weather, EPDM becomes less flexible. Hail during cold weather
  can crack/fracture the membrane at the impact point.
- **Seam damage**: Hail near seams can break the adhesive bond or tape splice.

**Testing — core cuts:**
The definitive test for hail damage on single-ply roofs. Cut a 2"x2" square through membrane
and insulation to examine insulation compression depth. Photograph, measure, then properly patch.

### TPO
- Similar to EPDM: punctures, bruising, membrane fracture
- May show impact marks as lighter-colored stress spots
- Hot-air welded seams can crack or separate from hail impact
- Scrim (reinforcement mesh) may be visible at fracture points
- Aged/UV-degraded TPO is more brittle and susceptible

### PVC
- Becomes increasingly brittle with age as plasticizers migrate out
- Older PVC can shatter like glass from hail impacts
- Cracks may radiate from impact points
- Weld seams generally stronger than membrane — fracture adjacent to welds is common

### Scope for Single-Ply Hail Claims
- Spot repairs compromise membrane warranty and waterproofing system
- Multiple punctures or widespread bruising typically justifies full replacement
- Include: membrane, insulation, fasteners/adhesive, cover board, flashing details, pitch
  pockets, drains, edge metal — the complete system must be addressed

## Hail Damage — Clay & Concrete Tile

### Clay Tile (including Ludowici)
(Xactimate: Ludowici Historic $5,625/SQ material only — NYBI26)

**What to look for:**
- **Cracks**: Through-body cracks — clean breaks or star-pattern fractures from impact
- **Chips**: Corner or edge chips from glancing impacts
- **Surface spalling**: Glazed surface chips away at impact, exposing lighter clay body
- **Broken tiles**: Large hail breaks tiles, especially at thin areas (edges, S-curve centers)
- **Hairline cracks**: **CRITICAL technique — wet the tile surface.** Cracks become visible as
  water is absorbed into the crack line. Essential inspection method for clay tile.

**Pattern analysis:**
- Windward-facing slopes show more damage (directional pattern confirms storm)
- Damage concentrates on exposed high points (barrel crown on S-tile, flat face on flat tile)
- Compare damaged vs. sheltered slopes

### Concrete Tile
- Same indicators as clay but concrete is slightly more impact-absorptive
- Surface slurry coating cracks and spalls — look for exposed gray concrete at impact points
- Heavier and thicker than clay — requires larger hail to damage

### Claims Significance
- Individual tile replacement possible IF matching tiles available
- Ludowici and specialty tiles: 8-16 week lead times for custom orders
- Color matching is critical — new tiles won't match UV-faded existing
- Walking on tile roofs causes additional breakage — include collateral damage in scope
- If underlayment beneath tile is aged (common on 20+ year roofs), tile removal + underlayment
  replacement + tile re-installation is a massive scope item

## Hail Damage — Cedar Shake

### What to Look For
- **Splits along grain**: Hail can split cedar shakes along natural grain. Distinguish from
  natural splitting by freshness (lighter, un-weathered wood inside) and impact mark at split origin.
- **Impact dents/divots**: Depressions with compressed wood fibers at impact point. Fresh impacts
  show lighter-colored wood at the crush point.
- **Corner/edge breaks**: Hail knocks off corners/edges. Fresh breaks show un-weathered wood.
- **Punctures**: Large hail can punch through thin or aged cedar shakes.

### Distinguishing Hail from Natural Weathering
**Key test**: Is the exposed wood at the damage point LIGHTER than the weathered surface?
- **YES → RECENT damage** (storm)
- **NO (same gray color) → OLD damage** (pre-existing)

| Indicator | Hail Damage | Natural Aging |
|---|---|---|
| Exposed wood color | Lighter (fresh) | Same gray as surface |
| Split pattern | Impact mark at origin, random distribution | Follows grain, weathered edges |
| Surface | Compressed fibers at impact | Graying, curling, thinning |
| Distribution | Random, directional with storm | Uniform aging patterns |

### Claims Significance
- Cedar shake is increasingly expensive and may be restricted by fire codes
- New cedar won't color-match weathered existing shakes
- In fire code zones, damaged cedar may trigger code upgrade to Class A roofing — significant
  scope expansion
- Matching stain/treatment is nearly impossible — supports slope or full replacement

## Hail Damage — Vinyl Siding

### What to Look For
(Xactimate: Standard $7.55/SF | High grade $7.83/SF | Premium $8.21/SF — NYBI26)

- **Cracks**: Star or spider-web pattern radiating from impact point
- **Chips/holes**: Pieces knocked out from impact
- **Dents**: Visible depressions at impact point
- **Shattered panels**: In cold weather, vinyl becomes extremely brittle — shattering and cracking
  that would NOT occur in warm weather

### Temperature Effect on Vinyl — CRITICAL
**Always check temperature at time of reported hail event.**

| Temperature | Vinyl Behavior | Damage Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Above 60°F | Pliable, may absorb impacts | Dents without cracking |
| 40-60°F | Stiffer, cracks more readily | Cracks and chips |
| Below 40°F | **Brittle** — same hail that dents in summer will SHATTER | Shattering, holes, fractures |
| Below 20°F | Extremely brittle | Even small hail causes extensive damage |

Cold-weather hail damage to vinyl is often far more extensive than what an adjuster expects
from the reported hail size. **Document the temperature as part of storm data.**

### Pattern Analysis
- Damage follows directional exposure — windward walls show more
- Concentrates on flat exposed face between lock mechanisms
- Check ALL elevations — compare windward vs. leeward
- Document density per elevation (e.g., "West elevation: 15 impacts per 10' section")

### Matching Issues
- UV fading over time means new panels in SAME product/color will NOT match existing
- Save a damaged piece for color comparison — hold next to new stock to demonstrate mismatch
- Even if manufacturer still makes the color, faded siding is 2-5 shades lighter than new
- This supports full-side or full-house replacement under matching argument
  (See matching-arguments.md for full matching/diminished value argument framework)

## Hail Damage — Aluminum Siding

### What to Look For
(Xactimate: $12.47/SF — NYBI26)

- **Dents**: Aluminum does NOT spring back — every dent is permanent. Circular or oval depressions.
- **Creases**: Larger hail creates creased depressions rather than smooth dents.
- **Paint damage**: Baked-on factory paint may crack, chip, or flake at dent point, exposing
  bare aluminum.
- **Loose panels**: Severe impacts can bend locking mechanism.

### Documentation
- Count dents per section (per panel or per 10' section)
- Measure representative dent diameters with ruler
- Note paint condition at each dent (intact vs. cracked/chipped)
- Photograph with oblique lighting to show depression shadows
- Note direction-facing of each elevation with damage vs. without

### Claims Significance
- Aluminum siding is largely discontinued for new construction
- Matching profile, exposure width, texture, AND color is extremely difficult
- Factory-painted aluminum cannot be field-painted to match — baked-on finish differs from
  brush/spray paint
- Dented aluminum cannot be repaired in place — panel replacement is the only option
- These factors strongly support full-side or full-house replacement when matching is impossible

## Hail Damage — Cedar Shake Siding

### What to Look For
(Xactimate: $18.13/SF — NYBI26)

- Same as cedar shake roofing: splits, impact divots, corner breaks, punctures
- Fresh splits vs. weathered splits — use the color test (fresh = lighter wood)
- Impact compressions show compressed wood fibers at impact point

### Claims Significance
- New cedar won't match weathered existing cedar (golden vs. silver-gray)
- Stain matching is extremely difficult
- Full-side or full-house replacement warranted for appearance matching

## Hail Damage — Gutters, Downspouts, Fascia, Trim

### Why Gutters Are the BEST Corroborating Evidence
Gutter damage is the #1 corroborating evidence for any hail claim. Gutters are:
- Horizontal and fully exposed — catch falling hail directly
- Thin aluminum (.027-.032") — dent easily and permanently
- Visible from ground level — easy to photograph
- Objective — dents are **undeniable physical evidence**

**What to look for:**
- Dents on front face (visible from below)
- Dents on top edge/lip
- Dents inside trough (visible from ladder/roof)
- Dent diameter correlates with hail stone size — measure representative dents
- Dent density (count per linear foot) supports storm severity
- Compare directional exposure — west/south-facing typically show more damage

### Downspouts
- Check face exposed to hail direction
- Dents corroborate hail direction
- Typically dented on one face only (windward)
- 2"x3" downspouts dent more readily than 3"x4"

### Fascia Metal
- Dents on vertical exposed face
- Document density and size same as gutters
- Corroborates hail even when roof is harder to assess

### Soffit
- Faces DOWN — hail does NOT typically damage soffit
- If soffit damage present, it's likely wind (panels displaced/buckled) not hail
- Wind-damaged soffit: panels pulled from J-channel, buckled, or missing

## Hail Damage — Aluminum Window Wraps

### What to Look For
(Xactimate: Std $398.11 | Small $256.48 | Large $538.85 | XLarge $652.25 — NYBI26)

- Dents on flat surfaces of window trim wraps
- **SILL** (horizontal bottom) is most exposed to vertical hail — shows most damage
- Side jamb wraps may show dents on face exposed to wind-driven hail direction
- Header (top) may show dents but partially sheltered by wall above

### Documentation
- Photograph each window with dented wraps
- Close-up of representative dents showing size and density
- Note which elevations show damage vs. sheltered (directional pattern)
- Count dented windows per elevation

### Claims Significance
- **COMMONLY MISSED BY ADJUSTERS** — they focus on roof/siding but overlook trim metals
- ALWAYS check and document window wrap damage — adds scope and corroborates claim
- Wraps are line items separate from siding — additional scope/cost
- Cannot be "un-dented" — replacement required

## Wind Damage Indicators

### Asphalt Shingle Wind Damage
- **Lifted/creased shingles**: Wind lifts shingle edge, creates crease at nail line. Once creased,
  seal strip is broken and shingle is compromised even if it settles back flat.
- **Missing shingles**: Complete loss. Look for exposed nail heads, torn mat, empty area.
- **Broken seal strips**: Wind lifts shingle enough to break adhesive seal without fully lifting.
  Shingle appears intact but is no longer sealed. Test by gently lifting leading edge.
- **Ridge cap damage**: Most wind-vulnerable component (highest point). Missing, lifted, or
  creased pieces are strong wind indicators.
- **Partial tab loss**: Wind tears off portion of tab, especially on aged 3-tab where keyway
  slots create stress points.

### Metal Roofing Wind Damage
- **Lifted/peeled panels**: Wind gets under leading edge and peels panel back/up
- **Fastener pullout**: On exposed-fastener panels, screws pull through from uplift
- **Seam separation**: Severe wind can separate snap-lock standing seam connections
- **Ridge cap displacement**: Metal ridge caps blown off or displaced

### Siding Wind Damage
- **Displaced panels**: Vinyl or aluminum panels blown off or shifted from locked position
- **Buckled panels**: Wind pressure buckles panels inward or outward
- **Missing sections**: Complete panel loss
- **J-channel separation**: Wind pulls panels out of J-channel trim

### General Wind Pattern
- Follows prevailing storm wind direction
- Higher elevations and unsheltered faces show more damage
- Corners and edges experience higher wind loads (Bernoulli effect)
- Ridge and edge shingles more vulnerable than field
- Roof penetrations create turbulence increasing local wind damage

## Distinguishing Storm Damage from Wear & Aging

### This is the #1 Battleground with Insurance Carriers

**CRITICAL PRINCIPLE**: Pre-existing wear and aging do NOT negate covered storm damage. A
15-year-old roof with granule loss and some curling can STILL sustain covered hail and wind
damage. The carrier must pay for the storm damage.

### Hail Damage vs. Wear — Key Differences

| Characteristic | Hail Damage | Normal Wear/Aging |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern | Random but directional | Uniform across roof |
| Granule loss shape | Circular/oval at impact points | Gradual, diffuse in water channels |
| Mat condition | Fractured/bruised at impact (HAAG) | Intact but exposed from gradual erosion |
| Freshness | Sharp edges, lighter color at impact | Weathered, same color as surrounding |
| Location bias | More on windward slopes | Even or more on south/west (UV) |
| Soft metal corroboration | Gutters/metals also show dents | No dents on soft metals |
| Timing | Correlates with reported storm event | Gradual over years |

### Wind Damage vs. Wear — Key Differences

| Characteristic | Wind Damage | Normal Wear/Aging |
|---|---|---|
| Seal strip | Broken/peeled from wind lift | Dried/failed from UV and heat cycling |
| Crease pattern | Sharp crease at nail line from lift | Gradual curling from edges inward |
| Location | Random or following wind direction | Uniform, or south/west concentrations |
| Surrounding shingles | Mix of damaged and undamaged | Uniformly deteriorated |
| Timing | Sudden, correlates with storm | Gradual over time |
| Missing shingles | Torn mat/torn nail holes visible | Clean release from failed adhesive |

### Blistering vs. Hail Damage

**Blistering**: Gas pockets trapped during manufacturing expand from heat, creating raised
bubbles. When the blister pops, it looks like a crater with granule loss.

| Indicator | Blistering | Hail Damage |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | Raised edges (volcano-like) | Depression/concavity (pushed IN) |
| Outline | Irregular, follows gas pocket | Circular/oval, consistent with impact |
| Mat beneath | No compression/fracture | Fractured/bruised (HAAG functional damage) |
| Distribution | South/west exposures (heat) | Directional with storm pattern |
| Soft metals | No corroborating dents | Corroborating dents on gutters/metals |

### Mechanical Damage vs. Hail

| Indicator | Mechanical (foot traffic, tools) | Hail Damage |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | Irregular | Circular/oval, consistent size |
| Distribution | Concentrated on access paths, near edges/HVAC/satellite | Random within directional pattern |
| Soft metals | No corroborating dents | Corroborating dents on gutters/metals |
| Timing | No storm correlation | Matches reported storm event |

### Algae, Moss, Lichen — NOT Hail Damage
Biological growths are cosmetic/maintenance issues, not storm damage. However, their PRESENCE
does NOT negate storm damage. Document separately and note they are pre-existing conditions
unrelated to the claimed storm damage.

## Collateral & Soft Metals Test Points

### Why Soft Metals Matter
Soft metal components provide **OBJECTIVE, UNDENIABLE** evidence of hail. A carrier can argue
about whether a dark spot on a shingle is hail or blistering — but they cannot argue that a
dent in an aluminum gutter appeared spontaneously.

### 12-Point Corroboration Checklist — Always Check and Document
1. **Gutters** — front face, top edge, inside trough
2. **Downspouts** — windward-facing surface
3. **Fascia metal** — vertical exposed face
4. **Aluminum window wraps** — sill, jambs, header
5. **Aluminum siding** (if present)
6. **Metal flashing** — drip edge, valleys, chimney counter flashing
7. **Outdoor HVAC condenser unit** — top and windward side
8. **Mailbox** (if metal, on property)
9. **Metal fence posts, light poles**
10. **Garage door** (metal doors show hail dents)
11. **Vehicles** (if parked outside during storm)
12. **Window screens** — hail can tear or dent metal screens

### Ground-Level Test Points (Before Climbing the Roof)
Check these from ground level first:
- Gutters visible from ground
- Window wraps (walk all four sides)
- HVAC condenser unit
- Garage door
- Mailbox
- Cars (if applicable)

**Ground-level soft metal damage CONFIRMS hail occurred** and supports the claim before the
roof inspection even begins. Start documentation with these items.

## Documentation Best Practices

### Photo Documentation Protocol

**For every inspection, capture:**
1. **Address/overview shot**: Front of property with address visible
2. **Four-side overview**: Property from all four elevations
3. **Roof overview**: Wide shot of each roof slope
4. **Test squares**: 10x10 representative areas with hits circled in chalk
5. **Close-ups of damage**: Individual damage points with ruler or coin for scale
6. **Soft metals**: All metal components showing hail damage
7. **Installation details**: Valleys, flashing, ridge, ventilation — anything affecting scope
8. **Age/condition**: Pre-existing wear (document separately from storm damage)
9. **Directional pattern**: Windward vs. leeward comparison showing differential concentration

### Annotation Language — Forensic Tone
When writing photo annotations for claim_config.json, use these patterns:

**For hail damage:**
> "Circular granule displacement with underlying mat fracture consistent with hail impact.
> Impact diameter approximately [X] inches. Per HAAG Engineering standards, mat fracture
> constitutes functional damage requiring replacement."

**For wind damage:**
> "Shingle exhibits sharp crease at the nail line with broken seal strip adhesion, consistent
> with wind-lift damage. The compromised seal strip renders this shingle non-functional
> regardless of whether it settled back to its original position."

**For soft metal corroboration:**
> "Multiple circular dents measuring approximately [X] inches in diameter observed on [component].
> Dent pattern and density corroborate hail occurrence reported on [date of loss]."

**For wear/aging (documented separately):**
> "Pre-existing granule erosion and UV degradation observed, consistent with normal aging of a
> [X]-year-old [material type] roof system. Note: pre-existing wear does not negate covered
> storm damage that is also present."

### Storm Data Integration
Always cross-reference damage findings with HailTrace or NWS storm data:
- Confirm reported hail size matches damage severity
- Confirm storm direction matches damage directional pattern
- Note any discrepancies and explain (e.g., "storm data shows 1.5" hail from the west;
  damage concentration on west-facing slopes is consistent with this data")
