## 1. Pipe Boot / Collar Failure

### Diagnosis Clues
- Cracked, split, or deteriorated neoprene collar around pipe penetration
- UV degradation causing rubber to become brittle and crack
- Visible gap between collar and pipe
- Water staining on ceiling directly below pipe penetration
- Pipe boot is the **#1 cause of roof leaks** on otherwise sound roofs

### Typical Age at Failure
- Neoprene: 8-15 years (UV accelerates failure)
- Lead: 30+ years (rarely fails unless mechanically damaged)

### Repair Procedure
1. **Protection**: Tarps below work area
2. **Removal**: Lift shingles surrounding pipe boot (2-3 courses above, 1 below). Remove old pipe boot — it slides over the pipe.
3. **Inspection**: Check decking around penetration for water damage/rot. If soft, replace decking section before proceeding.
4. **Installation**: Slide new pipe boot over pipe. Position flat base under upper shingles, over lower shingles. Nail at corners of base (4 nails). Apply roofing cement around base edge.
5. **Reinstall**: Lay shingle courses back in proper order. Nail and seal.
6. **Cleanup**: Check from inside if accessible — confirm no daylight visible.

### Materials
- 1x pipe boot (neoprene or lead)
- 4x roofing nails
- 1x tube roofing cement
- 2-3x replacement shingles (if damaged during removal)

### Estimated Time
- Laborer: 1.5-2 hours
- Journeyman: 1-1.5 hours
- Technician: 45 min - 1 hour

### Estimated Cost: $250-$400

## 2. Step Flashing Failure (Wall-to-Roof Intersection)

### Diagnosis Clues
- Corroded, bent, or separated step flashing along wall-to-roof intersection
- Water staining on interior wall at or below roofline
- Rust streaks on siding near roofline
- Visible gaps between step flashing and wall
- Step flashing covered by siding — may need siding removed to access

### Root Causes
- Corrosion (galvanized steel rusting out)
- Original installation used too few pieces (should be one per shingle course)
- Nailed through face of flashing (punctures waterproofing)
- Sealant-only installation (no mechanical fastening)

### Repair Procedure
1. **Protection**: Tarps below work area. If siding present, protect siding.
2. **Removal**: Remove shingles along wall intersection (typically 3-4 courses out from wall). If siding covers step flashing, carefully remove bottom 2-3 courses of siding. Remove old step flashing pieces.
3. **Inspection**: Check wall sheathing and decking for water damage. Check for house wrap condition behind siding. If decking is soft, replace section.
4. **Installation**: Install ice & water barrier along wall intersection (6" up wall, 6" onto deck). Install new step flashing pieces — one per shingle course, 2" overlap. Each piece goes UNDER the siding and OVER the shingle below. Do NOT nail through the face — nail at top edge only.
5. **Reinstall**: Lay shingle courses back. If siding was removed, reinstall siding courses.
6. **Cleanup**: Inspect from inside if accessible.

### Critical Note on Sequence
**ALWAYS work from BOTTOM to TOP** when installing step flashing. Each upper piece overlaps the lower piece. Working top-to-bottom creates reverse laps that funnel water inward.

### Materials
- Step flashing pieces (aluminum 4x4): 1 per shingle course along wall (count before removing)
- Ice & water barrier: wall length × 1 LF
- Replacement shingles: 3-4 per course × number of courses
- Roofing nails

### Estimated Time
- Laborer: 3-4 hours (without siding removal) / 5-6 hours (with siding)
- Journeyman: 2-3 hours / 4-5 hours
- Technician: 1.5-2 hours / 3-4 hours

### Estimated Cost: $350-$600 (without siding) / $500-$900 (with siding)

## 3. Chimney Flashing Failure

### Diagnosis Clues
- Surface-mounted counter flashing (caulked to chimney face, not embedded in mortar)
- Separated apron flashing at chimney base
- Missing or deteriorated cricket/saddle behind chimney (chimney width > 30")
- Water staining on ceiling/wall adjacent to chimney
- Mortar joint erosion around flashing

### Root Causes
- Counter flashing surface-mounted with caulk instead of embedded in mortar joints (most common)
- Apron flashing not extending far enough onto roof surface
- No cricket behind wide chimneys — water pools and backs up
- Mortar deterioration around embedded flashing

### Repair Procedure (4-Component System)
Chimney flashing is a **4-part system**: apron (front), step flashing (sides), counter flashing (over steps), cricket (back, if chimney > 30" wide).

1. **Protection**: Tarps below. Scaffold or ladder at chimney.
2. **Removal**: Remove 3-4 courses of shingles around all sides of chimney. Remove old apron, step flashing, and counter flashing. If embedded, use angle grinder to cut mortar joints clean.
3. **Inspection**: Check chimney mortar condition. Check decking around chimney for rot. Check if cricket exists behind chimney (required if chimney width > 30").
4. **Installation — Apron**: Install ice & water around chimney perimeter. Bend and install apron flashing at front, extending 4" up chimney face and 4" onto roof deck.
5. **Installation — Steps**: Install step flashing up both sides, one per shingle course, overlapping. Each step extends 4" up chimney and 4" onto roof.
6. **Installation — Counter**: Cut mortar joints with angle grinder (1" deep). Insert counter flashing lip into joint. Pack with mortar mix. Seal with polyurethane caulk (NOT silicone — silicone doesn't adhere to mortar long-term).
7. **Installation — Cricket**: If chimney > 30" wide, install cricket/saddle behind chimney to divert water.
8. **Reinstall**: Lay shingle courses back around all sides.
9. **Cleanup**: Clean mortar debris. Inspect from inside.

### Critical Notes
- Counter flashing MUST be embedded in mortar joints — surface-mounted caulk fails within 2-5 years
- Use polyurethane sealant, not silicone, for mortar joint sealing
- If chimney mortar is severely deteriorated, tuckpointing may be needed before flashing (scope expansion)

### Materials
- Step flashing pieces: count per side of chimney
- Counter flashing: chimney perimeter LF
- Apron flashing: chimney front width
- Ice & water barrier: chimney perimeter × 1 LF
- Mortar mix: 1-2 bags
- Polyurethane sealant: 2 tubes
- Replacement shingles: 3-4 courses per side
- Cricket materials (if needed): plywood, ice & water, shingles

### Estimated Time
- Laborer: 5-7 hours
- Journeyman: 4-5 hours
- Technician: 3-4 hours

### Estimated Cost: $400-$800 (without cricket) / $700-$1,200 (with cricket)

## 4. Exposed / Backed-Out Nail Heads

### Diagnosis Clues
- Visible nail heads on shingle surface (may have rust ring around them)
- Nails "popping" up through shingles (thermal cycling pushes nails out)
- Pinpoint water stains on ceiling (small, concentrated)
- Common on 8-15 year old roofs, especially with hand-nailing

### Root Causes
- Nails driven into gaps between sheathing boards (no holding power)
- Underdriven nails that work loose over time
- Thermal expansion/contraction cycling
- Wrong nail length for shingle + underlayment stack

### Repair Procedure
1. **Locate**: Find all exposed nail heads in the affected area. Mark with chalk.
2. **Option A — Reseat**: If nail is still in solid wood, tap it flush with hammer. Apply roofing cement over head. Cover with matching granules.
3. **Option B — Pull & Replace**: If nail has no holding power, pull it. Move 2" to the side into solid wood. Drive new nail. Seal old hole and new nail head with roofing cement.
4. **Option C — Shingle Replacement**: If shingle is cracked/damaged around nail, replace entire shingle. Lift courses above, slide out damaged shingle, slide in new one, nail, and seal.

### Materials
- Roofing cement: 1 tube
- Roofing nails: small quantity
- Matching granules (optional for cosmetic)
- Replacement shingles: 1-3 as needed

### Estimated Time
- 15-30 minutes per nail (including shingle lifting)
- Typical job: 1-2 hours for 5-15 exposed nails

### Estimated Cost: $250-$450

## 5. Missing or Damaged Shingles

### Diagnosis Clues
- Visible missing shingles or tabs
- Exposed underlayment or decking
- Wind-lifted shingles with broken seal strip
- Cracked or split shingles from hail or impact
- Water staining below the missing section

### Repair Procedure
1. **Protection**: Tarps if active leak. Secure area.
2. **Removal**: Carefully lift the shingle course above the damaged area. Remove nails from damaged shingle. Slide out damaged shingle.
3. **Inspection**: Check underlayment and decking condition.
4. **Installation**: Slide new shingle into position. Align with adjacent shingles. Nail with 4 nails per shingle (6 in high-wind zones). Dab roofing cement on nail heads and under tab edges above.
5. **Seal**: Press seal strip area firmly. In cold weather, apply dab of roofing cement under each tab.

### Matching Notes
- New shingles will NOT match weathered existing shingles
- If exact product match unavailable, use closest available
- For 1-3 shingles, color difference is acceptable for a repair
- If large area or highly visible, discuss with homeowner

### Materials
- Matching shingles: quantity as needed
- Roofing nails: 4-6 per shingle
- Roofing cement: 1 tube

### Estimated Time
- 15-30 minutes per shingle
- Typical job: 1-2 hours

### Estimated Cost: $250-$400

## 6. Valley Leak

### Diagnosis Clues
- Water staining along interior ceiling following the valley line
- Debris accumulation in valley creating a dam
- Corroded or punctured valley flashing
- Shingles trimmed too close to valley center (less than 2" setback)
- Ice dam formation in valley (winter)

### Repair Procedure
**If debris dam (simple):**
1. Clear all debris from valley
2. Inspect flashing condition
3. If flashing intact, clean and done

**If flashing failure:**
1. Remove shingles from both sides of valley (3-4 courses back)
2. Remove old valley flashing
3. Install ice & water barrier full length of valley
4. Install new W-style valley flashing
5. Reinstall shingle courses on both sides with 2" setback from center

### Critical Note on Valley Types
- **Open metal (W-style)**: Easiest to repair. Metal visible, shingles trimmed back.
- **Closed-cut**: One side runs through, other side trimmed. Requires removing overlapping side.
- **Woven**: Both sides interlocked. CANNOT spot-repair — must unweave and re-weave both slopes (3-4 ft sections minimum). This significantly increases scope.

### Estimated Cost: $300-$500 (open metal) / $500-$900 (closed-cut or woven)

## 7. Vent Boot / Exhaust Vent Leak

### Diagnosis Clues
- Water staining near bathroom/kitchen exhaust location
- Cracked or separated vent boot collar
- Missing or deteriorated sealant around vent
- Similar to pipe boot failure but for larger exhaust vents

### Repair Procedure
- Same general approach as pipe boot (Section 1)
- May require larger shingle area due to vent base size
- Ensure exhaust duct is properly connected inside attic

### Estimated Cost: $250-$450

## 8. Skylight Flashing Failure

### Diagnosis Clues
- Water around skylight interior frame
- Staining on ceiling near skylight edges
- Visible gaps between skylight frame and roof surface
- Deteriorated sealant around skylight

### Repair Procedure
1. Remove shingles around skylight (2-3 courses all sides)
2. Remove old flashing kit
3. Apply ice & water barrier around skylight opening
4. Install new skylight flashing kit (manufacturer-specific if available)
5. Reinstall shingles
6. Seal perimeter with polyurethane sealant

### Note
- Skylight leaks can also be caused by failed skylight seals (not roof flashing)
- If the glass-to-frame seal is failed, the skylight itself needs replacement
- Check from inside: is water entering at the glass edge or the frame-to-roof edge?

### Estimated Cost: $400-$700 (flashing only) / $800-$2,000+ (skylight replacement)

## 9. Ridge Cap Failure

### Diagnosis Clues
- Lifted, missing, or cracked ridge caps along roof peak
- Wind-lifted ridge caps (most common on hip-and-ridge caps)
- Water entering at ridge line
- Visible nail heads along ridge

### Repair Procedure
1. Remove damaged ridge caps
2. Inspect ridge vent condition (if present)
3. Install new ridge caps overlapping in correct wind direction
4. Nail with 2 nails per cap, sealed with roofing cement
5. If ridge vent is damaged, replace section

### Estimated Cost: $250-$500

## 10. Ice Dam Damage

### Diagnosis Clues
- Water intrusion at eaves during/after winter weather
- Icicle formation at eave (indicates heat loss)
- Interior water damage at exterior wall line
- Damaged or missing ice & water shield at eaves

### Temporary Fix
1. Create channels in ice dam to allow meltwater to drain (calcium chloride in pantyhose, laid across dam)
2. Do NOT chop ice dam with tools — damages shingles and gutters
3. If active leak: tarp from above if accessible

### Permanent Fix
1. Improve attic insulation and ventilation (root cause)
2. Install ice & water barrier at eaves (minimum 24" past interior wall line — 2 courses)
3. Ensure soffit vents are not blocked by insulation

### Note
Ice dam repair is often a temporary + permanent two-phase approach. The temporary fix stops the immediate leak; the permanent fix prevents recurrence next winter.

### Estimated Cost: $150-$300 (temporary) / $1,000-$3,000 (permanent — insulation + I&W)

## 11. Temporary Tarp Installation

### When to Tarp
- Active leak with rain expected before repair can be completed
- Waiting for materials (special order pipe boot, matching shingles)
- Damage too extensive for immediate repair
- After-hours emergency — tarp tonight, repair tomorrow

### Procedure
1. **Size**: Tarp must extend minimum 4 feet past the damaged area on all sides
2. **Secure**: Use 2x4 boards screwed into the roof deck at tarp edges (NOT nails — nails tear through tarp)
3. **Direction**: Upper edge of tarp must go UNDER shingles above the damage. Lower edge folds OVER eave.
4. **Weight**: In high wind, add sandbags or additional 2x4 battens across tarp surface
5. **Duration**: Maximum 30 days. Tarp degrades in UV and can damage shingles beneath it.

### Materials
- Heavy-duty tarp (blue poly minimum 10mil, or better: silver/UV-resistant)
- 2x4 boards: cut to tarp width
- 3" deck screws: 1 per 12" along 2x4
- Roofing cement or spray adhesive for tarp-to-shingle seal at top edge

### Safety
- NEVER tarp in active lightning
- Wet tarps on pitched roofs are extremely slippery
- Two-person job minimum

### Estimated Cost: $250-$500

## Scope Expansion Triggers During Repair

These situations discovered during repair expand the scope. Flag to homeowner before proceeding.

| Discovery | Impact | How to Handle |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Rotted decking under removed shingles | Add decking replacement | Photograph, measure, add to work order |
| Missing ice & water at eaves | Code upgrade if re-roofing | Inform homeowner — required by code |
| Second layer of shingles discovered | Full tear-off required if re-roofing | Inform homeowner — may change repair to replacement |
| Extensive mold/moisture in sheathing | Structural concern | Stop work, recommend structural assessment |
| Chimney mortar severely deteriorated | Tuckpointing needed before flashing | Add masonry scope or refer to mason |
| Siding damage discovered behind flashing | Siding repair/replacement | Add siding scope |

## When Repair Becomes Replacement

Flag these conditions — the repair may not be viable:
- **Decking soft/rotted** in area larger than 4'×4' → structural concern
- **Multiple leak sources** across different sections → systemic failure
- **Shingles at end of life** (20+ years, granule loss, curling) → repair won't last
- **Discontinued shingle** and large area needs matching → can't match
- **3+ existing layers** → code requires full tear-off, repair triggers replacement
