# Installation Techniques Reference Guide

> Reference file for photo analysis during /new-claim workflow.
> Read this when analyzing CompanyCam photos to identify installation methods that affect scope.

## Valley Types

Valleys are where two roof slopes intersect and channel water. The valley type directly affects
repairability and scope of work.

### Closed Cut Valley
**Installation**: Both roof planes' shingles are installed across the valley. The first plane's
shingles extend fully across. The second plane's shingles are laid over the first and then cut
in a straight line 2" back from the valley centerline. No metal valley flashing is visible.

**Visual identification**: A clean, straight line running down the valley where the cut was made.
One side's shingles run continuously through; the other shows the trimmed edge.

**Repair implications**: To replace a damaged shingle on the OVERLAPPING side, simply remove and
replace. But to replace a shingle on the UNDERLYING side, you must remove the overlapping shingles,
replace the underlying shingle, then re-install the overlapping ones. This doubles labor and
material for valley-area repairs and often causes collateral damage (nail holes, broken seal strips,
bent/creased shingles from prying up).

**Scope impact**: Valley repairs on closed-cut valleys should include labor for removing and
re-installing overlapping shingles, plus replacement of collateral-damaged shingles.

### Woven (Weaved) Valley
**Installation**: Shingles from both roof planes are woven alternately across the valley — one
from the left, one from the right, overlapping each other. No cut line, no exposed metal.

**Visual identification**: Shingles alternating from both slopes in a basket-weave pattern. The
valley has a slightly thicker, layered appearance.

**Repair implications**: This is the MOST difficult valley to spot-repair. You cannot replace a
single shingle without unwinding the weave in both directions. Repairing even one shingle
typically requires removing and re-weaving a significant section of both slopes. **This is a
major scope-expansion point.**

**Scope impact**: Woven valley repairs should be scoped as valley re-weave sections (minimum 3-4
feet in each direction). This dramatically increases repair area vs. simple shingle replacement.

### Open Metal Valley (W-Style)
**Installation**: A pre-formed metal W-channel (or rolled flat metal) is installed in the valley
first. Shingles from both slopes are trimmed back 2-3" from the valley centerline on each side,
leaving the metal exposed in the center.

**Visual identification**: Visible metal channel running the valley with a raised center rib
(the "W" shape). Shingle edges visible on both sides, cut back from center.

**W-style vs. rolled**: W-style has a formed center rib (water diverter); rolled metal valley is
flat without the center rib (less effective but simpler).

**Materials**: Galvanized steel, aluminum, copper. May be factory-painted or bare metal.

**Repair implications**: Easiest valley to spot-repair because shingles don't cross the valley.
Individual shingles can be removed and replaced on either side without affecting the other. The
valley metal itself may also need replacement if dented by hail — separate scope item.

**Scope impact**: If valley metal is damaged, it must be removed (requires removing shingles along
both edges), replaced, and shingles re-installed. If only adjacent shingles are damaged, repair
is straightforward.

### California Cut Valley (aka Long Valley, Half-Weave)
**Installation**: Shingles from the LARGER roof plane extend fully through the valley. Shingles
from the smaller plane are cut along the valley line. The dominant plane is always the larger one.

**Visual identification**: Similar to closed-cut but the larger/steeper plane always runs through.

**Repair implications**: Same as closed-cut — access to the underlying layer requires removing
the overlapping shingles.

## Flashing Types

### Step Flashing
**What it is**: L-shaped metal pieces (typically 4"x4" or 5"x7") installed at each shingle course
where the roof meets a vertical wall (sidewall intersection). Each piece overlaps the one below,
creating a stair-step pattern under the siding.

**RCNYS code**: R905.2.8 / R903.2 — flashing required at wall/roof intersections.

**Proper installation**:
- Each piece overlaps the one below by at least 2"
- Horizontal leg sits on the roof deck/shingle; vertical leg goes up behind the siding
- Each piece nailed once at top of vertical leg, into wall sheathing
- Should be INVISIBLE when properly installed (covered by shingle above and siding)
- Kick-out flashing (diverter) at bottom transition directs water into gutter

**Common defects**:
- Not installed at all — siding caulked to roof surface (improper, WILL fail)
- Nailed through the face of the shingle (creates leak point)
- Missing kick-out flashing at bottom (causes wall water damage)
- Too short (less than 4" on either leg)
- Sealant used as substitute for proper step flashing

**Scope impact**: If step flashing needs replacement, the siding above it must be removed and
re-installed. This adds siding removal/re-installation to scope. Include siding pieces, house
wrap repair (RCNYS R703.1 / R703.2), and trim re-installation.

### Counter Flashing
**What it is**: Metal flashing covering the TOP edge of step/base flashing where it meets masonry
(chimneys, parapet walls, brick walls). Embedded into a mortar joint or reglet (saw-cut groove)
and folds down over the step/base flashing below.

**Proper installation**:
- Inserted minimum 1" into mortar joint or reglet
- Extends down at least 2" over step/base flashing below
- Sealed in mortar joint with mortar or high-quality masonry sealant
- Should have a hemmed (folded) bottom edge to direct water away

**Common defects**:
- Surface-mounted with sealant only (not embedded) — temporary repair, WILL fail
- Nailed/screwed to masonry face (creates leak paths)
- Insufficient overlap over step flashing
- Caulk as primary waterproofing instead of proper embedding

**Visual identification**: Look at mortar joints where chimney/wall meets roof. Proper: top edge
tucked into mortar joint with metal extending down. Improper: surface-mounted with visible sealant.

### Apron Flashing (Headwall Flashing)
**What it is**: Continuous L-shaped metal flashing where a roof plane meets a vertical wall
(headwall condition). Horizontal leg extends under shingles; vertical leg goes up behind siding.

**RCNYS code**: R703.4 — wall flashing required at all exterior wall openings.

**Proper installation**:
- Horizontal leg minimum 4" under shingle course
- Vertical leg minimum 4" up behind siding
- Bottom edge has hemmed drip
- No exposed fasteners on horizontal leg

### Chimney Flashing (Complete System)
A properly flashed chimney has FOUR components:

1. **Front apron flashing**: L-shaped metal across the front (downslope) of chimney
2. **Step flashing**: Along both sides (see step flashing section)
3. **Counter flashing**: Embedded in mortar joints on all three masonry-exposed sides
4. **Back cricket (saddle)**: Peaked diverter behind chimney (upslope) preventing water/debris
   damming. Required by code on chimneys wider than 30". Made from metal or wood framing + metal.

**Common defects**:
- No cricket behind chimney (extremely common on older homes — code violation if > 30" width)
- Counter flashing surface-mounted instead of embedded in mortar
- Base flashing not extended far enough under shingles
- Rusted/deteriorated flashing metal
- Cricket covered with shingles instead of metal (less durable)

**Scope impact**: If any part of chimney flashing needs replacement, evaluate the entire system.
Often most cost-effective to replace the complete chimney flashing system. Include mortar joint
work/tuckpointing if counter flashing must be re-embedded.

### Pipe Boot Flashing (Vent Pipe Flashing)
**What it is**: Flashing assembly around plumbing vent pipes penetrating the roof.

**Types**:
- **Lead pipe boot**: Soft lead base with collar hand-formed around pipe. Most durable.
- **Neoprene/rubber boot**: Rubber cone friction-fit around pipe on metal base plate. Most common.
  Rubber degrades from UV — cracks, splits, hardens over time. **#1 cause of leaks on sound roofs.**
- **Plastic boot**: Cheapest, least durable. Cracks from UV and temperature cycling.

**Claims relevance**: Failed pipe boots are maintenance/age, NOT storm damage. HOWEVER, if
damaged by hail or wind-driven debris, that IS covered. Also: if the roof is being replaced due
to storm damage, new pipe boots are a required related item — they cannot be re-used when
shingles are stripped and replaced.

### Drip Edge
**What it is**: L-shaped metal along eaves and rakes directing water off the roof edge into gutter.

**RCNYS code**: R905.2.8.5 — drip edge REQUIRED on asphalt shingle roofs.

**Proper installation**:
- At eave: installed UNDER the underlayment, top leg under shingle
- At rake: installed OVER the underlayment, top leg under shingle
- Extends 1/2" to 3/4" beyond fascia board
- Overlapped minimum 2" at joints

**Scope impact**: If existing roof doesn't have drip edge and is being replaced, adding drip
edge is a **code upgrade** that must be included in scope (RCNYS R905.2.8.5).

## Ridge and Hip Treatments

### Ridge Cap
**Types**:
- **Cut 3-tab caps**: Individual 3-tab shingles cut into thirds. Inexpensive but has exposed
  nails and no clean dimensional look. Xactimate: $10.76/LF (NYBI26)
- **Manufactured hip & ridge**: Factory-made pieces (CertainTeed Shadow Ridge, GAF
  TimberCrest/Timbertex, OC DecoRidge). Thicker, color-matched, more wind-resistant.
  Xactimate: $11.97/LF (NYBI26)
- **Metal ridge cap**: Used on metal roofing systems. Formed to match panel profile.

**Wind damage**: Ridge cap is the most vulnerable component to wind — sits at the highest point.
Lifted, creased, or missing pieces are strong wind indicators. If seal strip is broken on ridge
cap, those pieces are compromised even if they settled back down.

### Ridge Vent
**Types**:
- **Shingle-over ridge vent**: Rigid/flexible vent strip along ridge opening, covered by shingle
  ridge cap. Most common on modern roofs. Brands: Cobra, Off Ridge, AirVent.
- **Aluminum ridge vent**: Metal profile atop the ridge. Visible — not covered by shingles.
- **Rolled/filtered ridge vent**: Mesh/filter stapled over ridge opening with shingles over it.
  Older style, less effective.

## Fastening Patterns

### Proper Shingle Nailing
- **4 nails per shingle** (standard, slopes < 60°)
- **6 nails per shingle** (high wind zones or manufacturer requirement for warranty)
- Nails in manufacturer's designated nailing zone (reinforced strip area)
- Heads flush with surface — not overdriven (breaks mat) or underdriven (prevents seal adhesion)

### High-Nailing
**What it is**: Nails placed ABOVE the manufacturer's nailing zone — too high on the shingle.
Nail only catches the top edge of the shingle below instead of full overlap zone.

**Significance**: Dramatically reduces wind resistance. Shingles blow off at much lower wind
speeds when high-nailed. If wind damage is observed and high-nailing is present, the installation
defect contributed — but the carrier still covers the damage. The defective installation may
actually EXPAND scope because more shingles blew off than would have with proper nailing.

## Underlayment & Ice Shield

### Underlayment Types
- **Asphalt-saturated felt (#15 / #30)**: Traditional. #30 is heavier and more water-resistant.
  Xactimate: $48.12/SQ (NYBI26)
- **Synthetic underlayment**: Polymer-based, lighter, stronger. Brands: GAF FeltBuster,
  CertainTeed DiamondDeck, OC ProArmor. Current best practice.
- **Ice and water shield**: Self-adhering rubberized membrane. Required by RCNYS R905.1.2 at
  eaves (min 24" inside exterior wall line) where avg daily January temp ≤ 25°F, in all valleys,
  and around penetrations. Xactimate: $2.24/SF (NYBI26)

### CRITICAL — Felt Underlayment Formula
**Felt (SQ) = Total Roof Area (SQ) - Ice & Water Barrier (SF ÷ 100)**

Felt and I&W both cover the roof deck but do NOT overlap. Felt covers only areas where I&W is
NOT installed. I&W goes at eaves (typically 2 courses x 6 ft) and valleys; felt covers the rest.

Example: 20 SQ roof with 1,000 SF I&W = 20 - 10 = **10 SQ felt**, NOT 20 SQ
Verification: felt qty + (I&W SF ÷ 100) should approximately equal total roof area.

### Code Requirements When Re-Roofing (RCNYS)
- **R905.1.2**: Ice and water shield at eaves (min 24" inside exterior wall line) where avg daily
  January temp ≤ 25°F
- **R905.1.2**: Ice and water shield in ALL valleys
- **R905.1.2**: Ice and water shield around all penetrations
- **R905.1.1**: Minimum one layer of underlayment over entire deck
- **R905.2.8.5**: Drip edge required at eaves and rakes
- These apply even if the original roof didn't have them — they are **code upgrades** that must
  be added during replacement and included in scope

### Two-Layer Rule (RCNYS R908.3.1.1(3))
Where a roof has two or more applications of any type of roof covering, ALL existing layers must
be removed before re-roofing. This converts ANY repair (even a few shingles) into a full
replacement when 2+ layers exist. The carrier CANNOT scope a repair over two layers.

### Deteriorated Decking (RCNYS R908.2)
Roof decking must be sound and capable of supporting the roof system. Deteriorated decking MUST
be replaced regardless of what caused the deterioration. The code asks about **current condition**,
NOT cause — carrier cannot deny decking replacement by arguing "pre-existing."

## Ventilation Systems

### Types
- **Soffit vents**: Intake vents under eave overhang. Perforated aluminum or individual strips.
- **Ridge vent**: Exhaust vent along roof ridge (see above).
- **Box vents (turtle vents)**: Static exhaust near ridge. Limited area each — multiple needed.
- **Turbine vents (whirlybirds)**: Wind-powered rotating exhaust. More effective than box in wind.
- **Powered attic ventilator (PAV)**: Electric fan exhaust. Can depressurize attic and draw
  conditioned air from living space — can actually cause problems.
- **Gable vents**: Vents in gable end wall. Function as intake/exhaust depending on wind direction.

### Ventilation Balance
**RCNYS R806**: Minimum 1 sq ft NFA per 150 sq ft attic space.

Proper ventilation requires balance between intake (soffit) and exhaust (ridge/box/turbine):
- 1:150 ratio with balanced intake/exhaust
- 1:300 ratio acceptable when balanced with vapor barrier present
- **Do not mix exhaust types** (e.g., ridge vent + box vents) — short-circuits airflow

## How Installation Method Affects Scope

### Scope Expansion Triggers

| Installation Detail | Why It Expands Scope | RCNYS Code |
|---|---|---|
| Woven valley | Cannot spot-replace; must re-weave section | — |
| Closed-cut valley (underlying side) | Must remove overlapping shingles; collateral damage | — |
| Step flashing behind siding | Siding removal/re-install + house wrap repair | R703.1, R703.2, R903.2 |
| Counter flashing in mortar | Mortar joint work; possible tuckpointing | — |
| No cricket on wide chimney | Code upgrade — add cricket during replacement | R903.2 |
| No drip edge on existing roof | Code upgrade — add during replacement | R905.2.8.5 |
| No ice & water shield (in code areas) | Code upgrade — add during replacement | R905.1.2 |
| High-nailed shingles | Explains excessive wind damage; documents defective installation | — |
| Discontinued product | Cannot match; triggers slope or full replacement | — |
| Mixed ventilation types | May require ventilation correction during re-roof | R806 |
| Two or more roof layers | ALL layers must be removed before re-roofing | R908.3.1.1(3) |
| Deteriorated decking | Must be replaced regardless of cause | R908.2 |
| House wrap at siding corners | WRB must wrap corners — forces full elevation coverage | R703.1, R703.2 |

### Collateral Damage Documentation
When repairing one component requires disturbing another, document and scope both:
- Replacing step flashing → siding removal and re-installation
- Replacing chimney flashing → counter flashing removal from mortar → mortar repair
- Valley repair → shingle removal on adjacent slopes
- Replacing decking → all shingles above must be removed and re-installed
- Ice & water shield installation → additional labor for full-adhered membrane vs. simple felt
