Executive Summary</h2> <p>On March 15, 2025, the property at 2212 Sheffield Ct, Owensboro, KY 42301, USA sustained storm damage affecting multiple exterior material systems. This two-story colonial features a brick veneer first floor, vinyl siding on the upper stories and gable ends, architectural laminated composite shingle roofing, and diamond-pattern glazed windows on the first-floor elevations. A 25-photograph forensic inspection documented damage across the roof system, vinyl siding on multiple elevations, first-floor window assemblies, and the porch ceiling, confirming that the March 15, 2025 storm event was the proximate cause of the documented losses.</p> <p>The inspection identified storm-induced damage to the second-floor vinyl siding on both the left and right elevations, including panel displacement, warping, buckling, and direct impact damage consistent with wind-driven forces. Water staining and discoloration on upper siding courses near the roofline, along with active water intrusion damage to the porch ceiling, confirm a storm-related roof breach that allowed moisture penetration into the porch structure. Four first-floor diamond-pattern windows — comprising two twin casement units and two picture window units — exhibit aged, deteriorated glazing and frame conditions that preclude repair-in-kind following the storm event.</p> <p>Full wall replacement of the affected vinyl siding is technically warranted because the existing product has been discontinued by the manufacturer, and replacement panels cannot achieve an acceptable color or profile match to the aged, weathered originals — a condition that triggers like-kind-and-quality restoration requirements under the IRC and applicable KRC provisions. Similarly, the four diamond-pattern windows cannot be re-glazed: the original glazing compounds and muntin profiles are incompatible with modern materials, and the aged sash assemblies will not accept new glazing without risk of seal failure. These conditions render spot repair infeasible and require full unit replacement to restore the dwelling to its pre-loss condition.</p> <p>The carrier's approved scope at $27,364.56 does not account for several documented damage items, including full wall siding replacement on the affected second-story elevations, full window unit replacements where glazing-only repair was confirmed infeasible, porch ceiling repainting necessitated by water intrusion from the storm-related roof leak, and the associated high-access charges for second-story work.
