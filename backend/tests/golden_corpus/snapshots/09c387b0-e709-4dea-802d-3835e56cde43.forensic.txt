Executive Summary</h2> <p>On March 13, 2026, the property at 9130 Avon Lake Rd, Lodi, OH 44254, USA sustained storm damage from a documented wind event. The subject property features an architectural laminated comp shingle system on the main dwelling and an older 3-tab asphalt shingle system on the shop structure. An 11-photograph forensic inspection was conducted to document the type, extent, and causation of wind damage across all roof elevations, with particular focus on the rear slopes of both structures where damage concentration was most severe.</p> <p>The rear elevations of both the dwelling and the shop exhibit widespread wind damage characterized by missing shingles, exposed RhinoRoof U20 synthetic underlayment, and bare roof deck. Close-range inspection of remaining shingles on the rear slope reveals sharp horizontal wind creases at the nail line with mechanically broken seal strips exhibiting clean separation — forensic indicators consistent with forcible wind uplift rather than age-related adhesive degradation. An exposed fastener with no oxidation corroborates recent storm-period timing, and the directional concentration of damage on the rear (windward) elevation, with the front elevation remaining largely intact, confirms a coherent wind-load pattern consistent with the March 13, 2026 event.</p> <p>The damaged 3-tab shingles are an older discontinued product with a nominal exposure dimension that cannot be matched by any currently manufactured metric shingle; spot repairs would produce visible exposure misalignment and fail to restore a uniform weather-resistant surface, rendering piecemeal repair technically infeasible. Under the Residential Code of Ohio (RCO), referencing IRC Section R905.2, replacement roofing materials must provide equivalent weather protection, and mixed installations of mismatched shingle profiles compromise both aesthetic uniformity and functional water-shedding performance.
