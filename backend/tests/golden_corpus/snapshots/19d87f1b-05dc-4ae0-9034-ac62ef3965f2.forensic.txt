Executive Summary</h2> <p>On July 03, 2025, the property at 13 Chrisfield Ave, Johnson City, NY 13790, USA sustained storm damage affecting multiple material systems across the structure. Our forensic inspection documented conditions across 61 photographs, evaluating architectural laminated composite shingles, EPDM flat roof membrane, metal flashings and coping, skylight assemblies, gutters, window screens, and the garage door. The damage profile is consistent with a hail event of sufficient intensity to compromise roofing, sheet metal, and auxiliary building components across all elevations.</p> <p>Chalk testing performed across the flat roof membrane field confirmed multiple hail impact zones exhibiting circular gaps in chalk lines — a definitive indicator of concave dent depressions in the membrane surface. Metal coping and cap flashing displayed fractured coatings with exposed reinforcement scrim, confirming impacts that penetrated the protective surface layer. Architectural laminated shingles documented at 10+ hail impacts per test square across multiple slopes, with visible granule displacement and mat bruising at each chalk-circled strike — well exceeding the accepted threshold for full slope replacement. Supplemental damage was confirmed on skylight mullion framing, roof vent caps with bare-metal exposure, gutter downspouts with hail punctures, and garage door panels exhibiting dense impact denting with paint disruption across all sections.</p> <p>The density and distribution of confirmed hail impacts across the entire roof system — membrane, shingles, flashings, coping, and penetration accessories — preclude spot repair as a viable remediation strategy. EPDM membrane with exposed scrim at impact sites has suffered irreversible loss of weatherproofing integrity, and architectural laminated shingles with mat bruising cannot be restored to pre-loss condition through localized treatment. Furthermore, replacement of damaged sections triggers material-matching and code-compliance obligations under the NJUCC and applicable IRC provisions, including requirements for underlayment, flashing integration, and fastener patterns that cannot be met through patchwork repairs to an aged roof assembly. Full system replacement is the only technically sound and code-compliant remediation path.</p> <p>The carrier's current scope at $441,131.95 does not appear to fully account for several documented damage categories, including EPDM membrane replacement with exposed reinforcement scrim at hail impact sites, metal coping and cap flashing replacement where protective coatings have been fractured through to substrate, skylight frame and flashing remediation, garage door panel replacement, and code-required upgrades triggered by the scope of roof system replacement under the NJUCC.
