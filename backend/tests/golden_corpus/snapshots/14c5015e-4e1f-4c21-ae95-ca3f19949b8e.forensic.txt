SECTION 2: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY --> <div style="margin-top:24pt;"></div> <h2>2. Executive Summary</h2> <p>On June 20, 2025, a high-wind storm impacted the property at 972 132nd Ave SE, Hope, ND 58046, USA, causing damage to multiple building systems across the site. The property comprises a large two-story residence clad in stone and vinyl siding with an architectural laminated composition shingle roof over OSB decking, as well as a Quonset-style outbuilding featuring an aged asphalt shingle roof on a curved steel frame with tongue-and-groove decking and boxed steel siding. Our forensic inspection, supported by 17 field photographs, evaluated the roofing, siding, ventilation components, flashing assemblies, and accessory structures for wind-related damage consistent with the reported storm event.</p> <p>The inspection documented directional wind damage across both the primary residence and the Quonset structure. On the main dwelling, a metal box vent exhibits wind-driven component failure with compromised seal integrity at the flashing-to-shingle interface, and the satellite dish sustained displacement consistent with high-wind loading. The Quonset building's curved roof profile—which accelerates airflow over the apex and generates amplified uplift forces—shows wind damage to its 16–20-year-old shingle system installed without ice and water shield protection. Boxed steel siding panels across multiple elevations display wind-induced displacement, mechanical separation at panel joints, and impact deformation with clear directional patterning consistent with the June 20, 2025 wind event.</p> <p>Full replacement of both roofing systems and the damaged steel siding is technically warranted based on three converging factors. First, the aged shingle systems—11–15 years on the residence and 16–20 years on the Quonset structure—cannot be spot-repaired to a weathertight condition because replacement shingles cannot achieve a color, granule profile, or weathering match to the existing field, creating differential performance zones that compromise long-term water shedding per IRC Section R905.2 requirements for weather-resistant coverings. Second, the Quonset structure's felt-only underlayment at the eaves fails to meet current IRC Section R905.1.1 standards for ice barrier protection in ND's climate zone, triggering code-compliance upgrades upon any roof replacement scope. Third, the mechanical separation observed in the boxed steel siding panels has compromised the interlocking joint geometry, rendering panel-by-panel repair impracticable without propagating further displacement to adjacent intact sections.</p> <p>The carrier's recognized scope at $936,363.53 does not appear to fully account for several documented damage categories, including the Quonset structure's full roof replacement with code-required ice and water shield underlayment upgrades, the satellite dish replacement, and the full extent of boxed steel siding panel replacement necessitated by wind-driven mechanical separation across multiple elevations.
